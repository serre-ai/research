# Credibility & Visibility Strategy

How Oddur Sigurdsson and Deepwork Research earn recognition in the ML/AI research community — without traditional academic credentials.

---

## The Challenge

Top ML venues (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR) are credential-heavy environments. PhDs, university affiliations, and lab pedigrees carry implicit weight. But the field has a strong counter-tradition: some of its most impactful contributors built credibility through output alone.

**The good news:** Double-blind review means your work is judged on merit, not affiliation. When ICLR switched to double-blind review, scores for prestigious authors actually decreased — the playing field leveled. Newcomers are roughly 2x more common at double-blind venues than single-blind ones.

**The core strategy:** Let the work speak. Build credibility through a sustained pipeline of high-quality, publicly visible research output — papers, tools, benchmarks, blog posts — until the name "Oddur Sigurdsson" and "Deepwork Research" become synonymous with rigorous, novel AI research.

## Precedents: Researchers Who Did It Without Credentials

### Chris Olah (Anthropic)
- Dropped out of University of Toronto at 18. Thiel Fellowship at 20.
- Strategy: Technical blog with clear visual explanations of neural networks. Found a "Pareto frontier" — the intersection of ML knowledge and visual communication where he had no competition.
- Path: Blog → Google Brain → OpenAI → Co-founded Distill.pub → Anthropic.
- Lesson: **Find your unique intersection. Make one thing clearer than anyone has before.**

### Gwern Branwen (Independent)
- Autodidact. No formal credentials. Lives on ~$12K/year.
- Strategy: Sustained, high-quality output over years. 538 citations on Google Scholar. Among the first to predict LLM scaling properties.
- Philosophy: "Argument screens off authority" — reasoning quality matters more than who you are.
- Lesson: **Consistency and intellectual rigor compound over years.**

### Andreas Madsen (Independent → PhD)
- Independent researcher in Denmark, no affiliation.
- Got an ICLR 2020 **spotlight paper** (top 6%) after 7-8 months of solo work with zero funding.
- Paper improved DeepMind's Neural Arithmetic Logic Units by 3-20x.
- The spotlight opened doors: podcast appearances, eventually a PhD position.
- Lesson: **One excellent paper at a top venue changes everything.**

### Jeremy Howard (fast.ai)
- Philosophy degree. No PhD. McKinsey consultant for 8 years.
- Path: Top Kaggle competitor → President of Kaggle → Founded Enlitic → Created fast.ai.
- Lesson: **Practical results (competitions, tools, education) can outweigh credentials.**

### Alec Radford (OpenAI)
- Never completed his degree at Olin College. Published DCGAN as a student, joined OpenAI in 2016.
- Lesson: **A single widely-used contribution (DCGAN, then GPT) establishes permanent credibility.**

### Sholto Douglas (Google DeepMind)
- BS in engineering/business. No masters or PhD.
- Worked on personal research nights/weekends (10pm-2am), posted on a blog, caught the attention of researchers at Google.
- Lesson: **Public work gets noticed. Ship consistently.**

## Positioning: Deepwork Research

### Affiliation
List **"Deepwork Research"** as institutional affiliation on all papers, arXiv submissions, and conference profiles. This is standard practice — independent researchers commonly use their own entity name. No verification exists at submission time.

Consider registering as a formal entity (LLC or nonprofit) for additional legitimacy when engaging with conferences, reviewers, and collaborators.

### What Makes a Small Lab Credible

**Do:**
- Publish citable research (arXiv, conferences, journals)
- Release open-source tools, benchmarks, and datasets that others use
- Maintain a professional website with clear research agenda
- Be transparent about size — don't pretend to be larger than you are
- Let output quality speak for itself

**Don't:**
- Misrepresent credentials, partnerships, or capabilities
- Use inflated "we" language without transparency
- Over-position with grandiose claims that don't match verifiable output

### Models to Emulate
- **EleutherAI**: Started as 3 people on Discord (2020). Built credibility through open-source models (GPT-J, GPT-NeoX, The Pile). Models downloaded 25M+ times. Published at NeurIPS, ACL, ICLR.
- **Epoch AI**: Data-first research nonprofit. Called "the best-researched survey work in the industry." Database called "an invaluable resource for policymakers" by RAND Corporation.
- **Distill.pub**: Peer-reviewed interactive ML journal. Registered with Library of Congress. Articles appear in Google Scholar. Co-founded by Chris Olah.

### The Deepwork Angle
The AI-augmented research platform itself is a unique angle. Lean into it:
- The meta-narrative — an independent researcher using AI tools to produce top-venue research — is inherently interesting.
- Write about the methodology of AI-assisted research, not just the research itself.
- This positions Deepwork Research at a novel intersection (like Olah's ML + visualization).

## Publication Strategy

### Venue Ladder (progressive difficulty)

**Tier 1 — Build the record (Months 1-4):**
- **arXiv preprints**: Establish priority, build visibility. Requires endorsement from an existing arXiv author in your category.
- **TMLR** (Transactions on Machine Learning Research): Rolling submissions, no deadlines. Reviews in 4 weeks, decisions in 2 months. Emphasizes correctness over flashiness — ideal for novel work. Double-blind on OpenReview.
- **Workshop papers** at NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR: 2-4 page extended abstracts, higher acceptance rates than main track, still carry prestige. Excellent for networking.

**Tier 2 — Establish presence (Months 4-8):**
- **AAAI**: Acceptance rate 15-25%, large and respected.
- **AISTATS**: ~31% acceptance, more theoretical/statistical focus.
- **CoLM** (Conference on Language Modeling): New venue, growing, less competitive.
- **JMLR**: Open-access journal, no page charges, high prestige.

**Tier 3 — Main event (Months 6-12):**
- **NeurIPS** main track: Submissions ~May, decisions ~September.
- **ICML** main track: Submissions ~January, decisions ~May.
- **ICLR** main track: Submissions ~October, decisions ~January.
- Having a workshop paper or TMLR publication gives credibility as a reviewer, which helps submissions.

### Submission Requirements

- **OpenReview profile**: Required for all major ML venues. Can list "Independent Researcher" as position. Profiles without institutional email go through moderation (up to 2 weeks).
- **Reviewer eligibility**: At least one author must be registered to review 3+ papers and must have at least one accepted publication at ICLR/NeurIPS/ICML or equivalent.
- **arXiv endorsement**: Needed for first submission. Find endorsers via the "Which of these authors are endorsers?" link on paper abstract pages.

### Pre-Submission Essentials
- Post to arXiv before or simultaneously with conference submission to establish priority.
- Release code on GitHub with clear documentation.
- Register on Papers With Code to link paper ↔ code.

## Visibility Pipeline

### Research Website
Build a professional site (deepwork.research or similar) with:
- **Research page**: Active projects, publications, and research agenda
- **Blog**: Technical posts explaining complex topics clearly (the Colah/Weng/Karpathy model)
- **Tools/Code**: Links to GitHub repos, benchmarks, datasets
- **About**: Honest positioning — independent research, AI-augmented methodology
- **Google Scholar** and **Semantic Scholar** profile links

### Blog Strategy
The blog is the highest-leverage credibility tool for an independent researcher.

**What to write:**
- Visual explanations of complex ML concepts (the Colah model)
- Accessible summaries of your own papers
- Deep dives into under-explained topics
- Methodology posts about AI-assisted research
- Literature syntheses that save readers time

**Distribution:**
- Share on Bluesky (primary — 300K+ academics migrated from X), Twitter/X, r/MachineLearning, Hacker News
- Target one high-quality post per 2-3 weeks
- Engage substantively with others' work in comments and replies

**Precedent impact:**
- Lilian Weng's Lil'Log started as learning notes → became one of the most authoritative ML blogs → 43,515 citations → VP of Research/Safety at OpenAI.
- Karpathy's "Unreasonable Effectiveness of RNNs" went viral → led to roles at Tesla and OpenAI.

### Social Media
- **Bluesky**: Primary platform. Academic ML has significantly migrated here. 27+ AI researcher starter packs. 83% of academics use it to collaborate/network.
- **Twitter/X**: Secondary. Still has reach but declining for academic content.
- **Reddit r/MachineLearning**: 3M members. Post papers with "We are the authors, AMA" engagement.

**Content cadence:**
- Paper threads (1-2/month when publishing)
- Research updates and visualizations (weekly)
- Substantive comments on others' work (daily engagement)

### Open-Source Artifacts
ML papers with GitHub repos see **~20% higher monthly citation rates**.

**High-leverage artifacts:**
1. Benchmark datasets with clear evaluation protocols
2. Evaluation frameworks that standardize comparison
3. Model implementations of papers lacking code
4. Tools that solve common ML pain points
5. Interactive demos (Hugging Face Spaces, Gradio)

**Goal:** Create something that others cite. Every user of your benchmark/tool is a potential citation.

## Community Integration

### Organizations to Join
- **ML Collective**: 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Free mentorship, compute, and collaboration for independent researchers. Office hours, research jams, paper reading groups.
- **EleutherAI Discord**: Open to anyone. Mentorship, guidance, computing resources.
- **Cohere Labs**: 4,500 members from 150 countries. Scholars Program with mentorship from top research teams.

### Conference Participation (Beyond Papers)
- **Workshop papers**: Lower barrier, same conferences.
- **Demo track**: 2-page submission, single-blind (no anonymization needed).
- **Poster sessions**: Present work in progress, get feedback.
- **Competitions**: Winning ML competitions builds credibility independent of credentials (the Jeremy Howard path).
- **Affinity groups**: NeurIPS has groups for many communities — good for networking.
- **NeurIPS Meetups**: 75 meetups in 35 countries. Local events using conference recordings.

### Reviewer Pathway
1. Start as workshop reviewer (lowest barrier)
2. Self-nominate as NeurIPS main track reviewer (experience needed)
3. Build toward Area Chair over several years
4. **NeurIPS self-nomination**: Anyone with experience can apply. For AC: ideally 5+ last-authored papers.

### Collaboration
- **Cold emails**: Chris Olah emphasizes that thoughtful, specific cold emails work. Spend time crafting them.
- **Conference networking**: Even without a paper, attend one major conference (NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR) for in-person connections.
- **Co-authoring**: Partnering with credentialed researchers on early papers lends credibility and helps learn venue norms.

## 12-Month Credibility Roadmap

### Months 1-3: Foundation
1. Set up all online profiles (checklist above)
2. Launch research website with blog
3. Write 2-3 high-quality technical blog posts with clear visualizations
4. Release first open-source artifact (benchmark, tool, or dataset)
5. Join ML Collective and EleutherAI communities
6. Get arXiv endorsement and post first preprint
7. Begin engaging on Bluesky — comment on papers, share insights

### Months 3-6: First Publications
1. Submit first workshop paper to NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR
2. Submit to TMLR (rolling — no deadline pressure)
3. Send thoughtful cold emails to researchers whose work you build on
4. Attend one major conference (even without a paper)
5. Grow to 500+ followers on Bluesky
6. Start reviewing for workshops
7. Post research updates weekly

### Months 6-9: Establish Presence
1. Submit to main track at NeurIPS, ICML, or ICLR
2. Release a significant open-source contribution (100+ GitHub stars target)
3. Give talks at local ML meetups
4. Pitch yourself to podcasts (TWIML, ML Street Talk, Latent Space)
5. Self-nominate as conference reviewer
6. Publish 2-3 more blog posts that demonstrate deep expertise

### Months 9-12: Recognition
1. Aim for first main-track acceptance
2. Build toward a signature contribution — one thing people associate with your name
3. Register Deepwork Research formally (LLC or nonprofit)
4. Professional website with 5+ publications, active blog, research tools
5. Present at conferences
6. 1,000+ followers, regular engagement, recognized in your subfield

## The Narrative

The story to tell — on the website, in bios, at conferences:

> Oddur Sigurdsson is an independent AI researcher and founder of Deepwork Research, investigating [your key areas]. His work focuses on [specific contributions]. He builds open-source tools for [purpose] and publishes at [venues].

Keep it simple. Let publications and tools fill in the credibility. The more specific the contributions listed, the more weight the bio carries.

**What not to say:** Don't apologize for lacking credentials. Don't over-explain your background. The work is the credential.

## Key Principles

1. **One excellent paper beats ten mediocre ones.** Quality over volume, always.
2. **The blog is your amplifier.** Every paper should have a companion blog post. Every insight should be shared publicly.
3. **Open-source is your citation engine.** Tools and benchmarks others use generate citations automatically.
4. **Community before asks.** Build relationships by engaging with others' work before requesting anything.
5. **Transparency over posturing.** Being openly independent is more credible than pretending to be a large institution.
6. **The meta-narrative is an asset.** An independent researcher using AI to produce top-venue research is a compelling story. Tell it.
7. **Rejection is expected.** At ~20% acceptance rates, plan for multiple submission cycles. Each rejection provides reviewer feedback that makes the next paper stronger.
8. **Double-blind review is your ally.** Your work is judged on its merits, not your affiliation. This is a structural advantage for outsiders.
