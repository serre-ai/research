\begin{abstract}
Large language models demonstrate remarkable reasoning abilities yet fail systematically on seemingly simple problems.
We show these failures are not random but correspond to \emph{reasoning gaps}---problem classes that fall outside the computational boundary of the model's architecture.
Building on transformer expressiveness results that place fixed-depth log-precision transformers within $\TC$ (constant-depth threshold circuits), we develop a six-type taxonomy of reasoning gaps, each grounded in a specific complexity-theoretic boundary: \emph{sensitivity} ($\AC$), \emph{depth} ($\TC/\NC$), \emph{serial composition} (linear depth), \emph{algorithmic} ($\PTIME$), \emph{intractability} ($\NP$), and \emph{architectural} (autoregressive constraints).
We introduce \textsc{ReasonGap}, a diagnostic benchmark suite of nine tasks designed to isolate each gap type, with testable predictions about when chain-of-thought reasoning, model scaling, and tool augmentation should close or fail to close each gap.
Evaluation across ten models from five families (134,572 instances) validates the taxonomy: chain-of-thought closes depth and serial gaps as predicted by complexity theory, tool use dominates for algorithmic gaps, and architectural gaps persist regardless of intervention.
Our framework provides both explanatory power for why models fail and practical guidance for which mitigation strategies to apply.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved striking performance on reasoning benchmarks spanning mathematics~\citep{gsm8k}, logical inference~\citep{bigbench}, and commonsense reasoning~\citep{commonsense}.
Yet these same models fail unpredictably on problems that appear trivially simple---miscounting objects in a list~\citep{alice}, collapsing under an irrelevant clause in a math problem~\citep{gsmsymbolic}, or failing to infer ``B is A'' from training on ``A is B''~\citep{reversal}.
These failures are not random.
They reveal \emph{systematic reasoning gaps} that correlate with structural properties of the underlying problem.

Two largely disconnected research communities have studied this phenomenon.
The \emph{complexity-theoretic} community has established that fixed-depth log-precision transformers are computationally bounded by $\TC$~\citep{merrill2022saturated,merrill2023parallelism}, with chain-of-thought (CoT) extending expressiveness up to $\PTIME$ with polynomially many intermediate steps~\citep{merrill2024cot,li2024cot}.
The \emph{empirical} community has catalogued dozens of failure patterns---in compositionality~\citep{dziri2023faith}, planning~\citep{kambhampati2024}, causal reasoning~\citep{joshi2024}, and mathematical reasoning~\citep{gsmsymbolic}---without connecting them to a formal framework.

We bridge this divide.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Formal definition} of \emph{reasoning gap} as a problem class outside the model's complexity-theoretic capability class (\Cref{sec:framework}).
    \item \textbf{Six-type taxonomy} of reasoning gaps, each grounded in a specific complexity boundary, with formal propositions characterizing existence and closure conditions (\Cref{sec:taxonomy}).
    \item \textbf{\textsc{ReasonGap} benchmark suite} of nine diagnostic tasks, each isolating a specific gap type with testable predictions about CoT, scaling, and tool-use effects (\Cref{sec:benchmark}).
    \item \textbf{Empirical validation} across ten models from five families, confirming the taxonomy's predictions (\Cref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

Our central thesis is that reasoning gaps are \emph{predictable}: given the complexity class of a problem and the computational class of a model, one can determine \emph{a priori} whether the model will exhibit a gap, what type of gap it will be, and which mitigation strategies (if any) will close it.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Circuit Complexity Classes}

We use the following standard complexity classes, ordered by inclusion:
\[
\AC \subseteq \TC \subseteq \NC \subseteq \LOGSPACE \subseteq \NL \subseteq \PTIME \subseteq \NP
\]

$\AC$ consists of problems decidable by constant-depth, polynomial-size circuits with unbounded fan-in AND and OR gates.
$\TC$ adds MAJORITY (threshold) gates.
$\NC$ allows $O(\log n)$ depth with bounded fan-in.
All inclusions are believed strict, though $\TC \neq \NC$ remains open.

Key separations relevant to LLM reasoning:
PARITY $\notin \AC$~\citep{furst1984,hastad1986};
Boolean formula evaluation is $\NC$-complete;
directed graph connectivity is $\NL$-complete;
3-SAT is $\NP$-complete.

\subsection{Transformer Expressiveness}
\label{sec:expressiveness}

A series of results has precisely characterized the computational power of transformers under various assumptions:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{No CoT, constant precision:} Transformers are bounded by $\AC$~\citep{li2024cot}.
    \item \textbf{No CoT, log precision:} Transformers are bounded by uniform $\TC$~\citep{merrill2022saturated,merrill2023parallelism}, equivalently expressible as first-order logic with majority quantifiers~\citep{merrill2023logic}.
    \item \textbf{$O(\log n)$ CoT steps:} Expressiveness extends to at most $\LOGSPACE$~\citep{merrill2024cot}.
    \item \textbf{$O(n)$ CoT steps:} All regular languages are recognizable~\citep{merrill2024cot}.
    \item \textbf{$O(n^c)$ CoT steps:} Expressiveness equals exactly $\PTIME$~\citep{merrill2024cot}.
\end{itemize}

These results are architecture-agnostic: state-space models and Mamba share the $\TC$ bound~\citep{mamba2024complexity}.
The limitation stems from constant-depth parallel computation itself, not the attention mechanism specifically---a phenomenon \citet{merrill2023parallelism} term the \emph{parallelism tradeoff}.

Crucially, expressiveness does not imply learnability.
\citet{hahn2024sensitive} show that functions with high Boolean sensitivity occupy isolated points in parameter space, making them unlearnable via gradient descent even when expressible.

\subsection{Empirical Reasoning Failures}
\label{sec:empirical_background}

Documented failure patterns include: performance decay with compositional depth~\citep{dziri2023faith}; sensitivity to irrelevant information~\citep{gsmsymbolic}; inability to plan~\citep{kambhampati2024}; the reversal curse~\citep{reversal}; causal reasoning fallacies~\citep{joshi2024}; and reasoning collapse beyond a critical chain length~\citep{limitedspace2025}.
\citet{song2026failures} provide a comprehensive taxonomy, but without complexity-theoretic grounding.

\section{Formal Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Definitions}

\begin{definition}[Reasoning Task]
\label{def:task}
A \emph{reasoning task} is a family $\calF = \{f_n : \Sigma^{\leq n} \to \Sigma^*\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ of functions parameterized by input size $n$, equipped with a decidable correctness criterion $V(x, y) \in \{0, 1\}$ such that $V(x, f_n(x)) = 1$ for all valid inputs $x$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Model Capability Class]
\label{def:capability}
Given a model class $\calM$ (e.g., depth-$d$ log-precision transformers), the \emph{capability class} $\calC(\calM) \subseteq 2^{\calF}$ is the set of reasoning tasks $\calF$ such that $\calM$ solves $\calF$ with probability $\geq 2/3$ on uniformly random instances of size $n$, for all sufficiently large $n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Reasoning Gap]
\label{def:gap}
A model class $\calM$ has a \emph{reasoning gap} on task $\calF$ if:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item $\calF \in \PTIME$ (the task is tractable);
    \item $\calF \notin \calC(\calM)$ (the task exceeds the model's capability class);
    \item The model's accuracy on $\calF$ decreases monotonically with input size $n$ (the failure is systematic, not random).
\end{enumerate}
A gap is \emph{structural} if condition (2) follows from a proven complexity separation, and \emph{empirical} if observed experimentally.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Gap Closure]
\label{def:closure}
An augmentation technique $\calT$ (e.g., CoT with budget $b(n)$, tool use, external memory) \emph{closes} a reasoning gap on $\calF$ for model $\calM$ if $\calF \in \calC(\calM + \calT)$.
A technique \emph{partially closes} a gap if it improves asymptotic accuracy without achieving a constant lower bound.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Reasoning Gap Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

We identify six types of reasoning gaps, each defined by the complexity boundary it reflects.
\Cref{tab:taxonomy} summarizes the taxonomy with predictions.

\paragraph{Type 1: Sensitivity Gap ($\AC$ boundary).}
Problems requiring high Boolean sensitivity, such as PARITY and exact counting.
By the Linial--Mansour--Nisan theorem~\citep{lmn1993}, functions with sensitivity $\Omega(n)$ are not in $\AC$.
Even short CoT ($O(\log n)$ steps) closes this gap for MAJORITY-type problems, but PARITY requires $\Omega(n)$ steps~\citep{bavandpour2025}.
Importantly, even if expressible, high-sensitivity functions may be unlearnable due to loss landscape geometry~\citep{hahn2024sensitive}.

\paragraph{Type 2: Depth Gap ($\TC/\NC$ boundary).}
Problems requiring super-constant circuit depth, outside $\TC$ conditional on $\TC \neq \NC$.
Boolean formula evaluation ($\NC$-complete) and arbitrary context-free language parsing are canonical examples.
Recent hardness results confirm this prediction: \citet{yehudai2026compositional} prove that transformers solving compositional reasoning problems require depth proportional to tree depth.
Accuracy degrades with \emph{nesting depth}, and $O(\log n)$ CoT steps should suffice for $\NC$ problems.

\paragraph{Type 3: Serial Composition Gap.}
Problems requiring $\Omega(n)$ sequential computation steps, such as iterated permutation composition and long-range state tracking.
These correspond to regular language recognition, which requires $O(n)$ CoT steps for constant-depth transformers~\citep{merrill2024cot}.
This is the gap type for which CoT is most precisely matched: the required CoT budget equals the number of serial steps.

\paragraph{Type 4: Algorithmic Gap.}
Problems in $\PTIME$ requiring non-trivial algorithms (shortest path, dynamic programming).
In principle, polynomial CoT suffices ($\calC(\calM + \CoT(n^c)) = \PTIME$), but the model must discover or learn the algorithm.
In practice, tool use (code execution) closes this gap far more reliably than CoT~\citep{gao2023pal}.

\paragraph{Type 5: Intractability Gap.}
$\NP$-hard problems where no polynomial-time algorithm exists (assuming $\PTIME \neq \NP$).
Even polynomial CoT reaches only $\PTIME$.
Accuracy degrades sharply at phase transitions (e.g., 3-SAT at $\alpha \approx 4.27$)~\citep{hazra2025}.

\paragraph{Type 6: Architectural Gap.}
Failures arising from the autoregressive structure, causal masking, or training objective---not from computational complexity.
The reversal curse~\citep{reversal}, negation insensitivity~\citep{negation2025}, and sensitivity to irrelevant information~\citep{gsmsymbolic} are examples.
These gaps persist regardless of CoT, scaling, or problem size.

\subsection{Formal Results}

\begin{proposition}[Sensitivity Gap Existence]
\label{prop:sensitivity}
Under standard circuit complexity assumptions, there exist reasoning tasks with Boolean sensitivity $s(f) = n$ that no constant-depth, polynomial-size circuit (and hence no constant-depth transformer) can solve for all $n$, regardless of width.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Depth Gap Existence]
\label{prop:depth}
If $\TC \neq \NC$, there exist reasoning tasks solvable in $\NC$ that no constant-depth log-precision transformer can solve, but $O(\log n)$ CoT steps suffice.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Serial Gap Characterization]
\label{prop:serial}
For iterated composition of $k$ functions from a finite domain of size $m$, constant-depth transformers require $\Omega(k)$ CoT steps. This bound is tight: $O(k)$ steps suffice.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[CoT Budget Monotonicity]
\label{prop:monotone}
For Type~3 gaps, model accuracy is monotonically non-decreasing in CoT budget up to the optimal budget $O(n)$, after which additional computation provides no further benefit and may degrade accuracy.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Intractability Invariance]
\label{prop:intractable}
For Type~5 gaps, accuracy at the $\NP$-hard phase transition does not improve beyond a constant factor with any polynomial CoT budget.
\end{proposition}

\Cref{prop:sensitivity,prop:depth} follow from known circuit lower bounds~\citep{furst1984,hastad1986} combined with the $\TC$ upper bound on transformers~\citep{merrill2022saturated}.
\Cref{prop:serial} follows from \citet{merrill2024cot} and \citet{li2024cot}.
\Cref{prop:monotone} is novel and connects to the ``overthinking'' phenomenon observed by \citet{mirage2025}.
\Cref{prop:intractable} follows from the assumption $\PTIME \neq \NP$ and the $\calC(\calM + \CoT(n^c)) = \PTIME$ characterization.

\section{The \textsc{ReasonGap} Benchmark Suite}
\label{sec:benchmark}
% ============================================================

We design nine diagnostic tasks, each targeting a specific gap type.
All tasks use procedurally generated instances with controlled difficulty parameters to avoid training data contamination.
\Cref{tab:tasks} summarizes the suite.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{\textsc{ReasonGap} benchmark tasks. Each task targets a specific gap type with a controlled difficulty parameter and a prediction from the taxonomy.}
\label{tab:tasks}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}clllll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{ID} & \textbf{Task} & \textbf{Gap Type} & \textbf{Complexity} & \textbf{Difficulty} & \textbf{CoT Prediction} \\
\midrule
B1 & Masked Majority & Sensitivity & $\TC \setminus \AC$ & String length $n$ & Short CoT closes \\
B2 & Nested Boolean Eval & Depth & $\NC$-complete & Nesting depth $d$ & $O(\log n)$ CoT closes \\
B3 & Iterated Permutation & Serial & $\Omega(k)$ serial & Compositions $k$ & $O(k)$ CoT required \\
B4 & State Machine Sim & Serial & $\Omega(k)$ serial & Transitions $k$ & $O(k)$ CoT required \\
B5 & Graph Reachability & Depth/Algo & $\NL$-complete & Nodes $n$ & Partial improvement \\
B6 & Longest Incr.\ Subseq & Algorithmic & $\PTIME$ & Sequence length & Tool use dominates \\
B7 & 3-SAT & Intractability & $\NP$-complete & Variables $n$ & No improvement \\
B8 & Reversal Inference & Architectural & Structural & Distractor count & No improvement \\
B9 & Negation Sensitivity & Architectural & Structural & Negation depth & Minimal \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{B1: Masked Majority.}
Given a binary string of length $n$ with randomly masked positions, determine the majority value among visible bits.
MAJORITY $\in \TC \setminus \AC$, so constant-precision transformers should fail at this while log-precision transformers may succeed on small instances.
CoT (explicit counting) should close the gap.

\paragraph{B2: Nested Boolean Evaluation.}
Evaluate a Boolean formula with nested AND/OR/NOT operations of depth $d$.
Boolean formula value is $\NC$-complete under $\AC$ reductions, so performance should degrade with nesting depth, not formula size.

\paragraph{B3: Iterated Permutation Composition.}
Apply $k$ permutations sequentially to an initial element.
Requires $\Omega(k)$ serial steps; performance should degrade linearly with $k$.

\paragraph{B4: State Machine Simulation.}
Simulate a finite automaton for $k$ steps.
Isomorphic to B3 in complexity requirements; included as a natural-language framing of the same serial computation.

\paragraph{B5: Graph Reachability.}
Determine if a path exists between two nodes in a directed graph.
$\NL$-complete; if $\TC \neq \NL$, this is outside transformer capability without CoT.

\paragraph{B6: Longest Increasing Subsequence.}
Find the LIS length of a random sequence.
In $\PTIME$ but requires dynamic programming; code execution should dramatically outperform CoT.

\paragraph{B7: 3-SAT at Phase Transition.}
Determine satisfiability at clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha \approx 4.27$.
$\NP$-complete; accuracy should drop sharply at the phase transition.

\paragraph{B8: Reversal Inference.}
Present facts as ``A is B'' and query ``what has property B?''
Tests the reversal curse~\citep{reversal}; gap should be constant regardless of intervention.

\paragraph{B9: Negation Sensitivity.}
Answer questions with increasing negation depth.
Tests insensitivity to logical negation; gap should increase with depth but not be closable by CoT.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
Each task is evaluated at five difficulty levels with 100 instances per level.
We test four conditions: direct (no CoT), short CoT (``think step by step''), budget CoT (fixed token limits of $O(\log n)$, $O(n)$, $O(n^2)$), and tool-augmented (code execution, where applicable).
Models span five families: Claude (Haiku~4.5), GPT (4o-mini, 4o, o3), Llama~3.1 (8B, 70B), Mistral (Ministral~8B, Small~24B), and Qwen~2.5 (7B, 72B).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ============================================================

We evaluate the \textsc{ReasonGap} benchmark across ten models from five families under three reasoning conditions, totaling 134,572 evaluated instances.
The results validate the taxonomy's central predictions: chain-of-thought selectively closes depth and serial gaps while leaving intractability and architectural gaps unchanged, and reasoning budget calibration critically determines whether constrained CoT helps or harms.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
We evaluate ten models spanning five families and two scale categories (\Cref{tab:models}).
Proprietary models include Claude Haiku~4.5 (Anthropic), GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, and o3 (OpenAI).
Open-source models include Llama~3.1 8B and 70B (Meta), Ministral~8B and Mistral~Small~24B (Mistral), and Qwen~2.5 7B and 72B (Alibaba).
Notably, o3 is a reasoning model with internal chain-of-thought, providing a natural test of whether built-in reasoning compensates for architectural limitations.
For scale analysis, we partition open-source models into \emph{small} ($\leq$10B: Llama~8B, Ministral~8B, Qwen~7B) and \emph{medium} (10--80B: Llama~70B, Mistral~Small~24B, Qwen~72B).

\paragraph{Evaluation conditions.}
Each task is evaluated under three conditions:
(1)~\textbf{Direct}: the model must answer immediately without intermediate reasoning (``Answer with just the final answer.'');
(2)~\textbf{Short CoT}: unconstrained chain-of-thought (``Think step by step, then provide your final answer.'');
(3)~\textbf{Budget CoT}: chain-of-thought with a fixed token budget calibrated per task.\footnote{The B2 budget was recalibrated from $O(2^d)$ (too restrictive at 24 words for depth~3) to a node-count formula giving ${\sim}180$ words. See \Cref{sec:budget} for discussion.\label{fn:b2budget}}

\paragraph{Tasks and instances.}
We use the nine \textsc{ReasonGap} diagnostic tasks described in \Cref{sec:benchmark}.
Each task is evaluated at five difficulty levels with 100 instances per level, yielding 500 instances per task per model per condition.
All instances are procedurally generated to avoid training data contamination.

\paragraph{Answer extraction.}
We use task-specific parsers to extract final answers from model responses.
Each parser includes refusal detection (identifying ``I cannot'' and similar patterns) and format normalization.
For multiple-choice tasks (B7, B8), we extract the selected option; for numeric tasks (B1, B3, B4, B6), we extract the final number; for Boolean tasks (B2, B5), we extract Yes/No.

\paragraph{Statistical methodology.}
All reported confidence intervals are 95\% bootstrap CIs computed from 10,000 resamples.
Paired comparisons between conditions use McNemar's test on the same instances.
We report significance at $\alpha = 0.01$ after Bonferroni correction across tasks.

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Effectiveness by Gap Type}
\label{sec:cot_results}

The central empirical finding of this paper is that \textbf{chain-of-thought effectiveness is strongly predicted by gap type}.

\subsection{Budget Sensitivity}
\label{sec:budget}

Budget CoT reveals that \textbf{reasoning budget calibration is critical}: a well-calibrated budget can approach unconstrained CoT performance, but a miscalibrated budget can perform \emph{worse} than no reasoning at all.

\paragraph{Well-calibrated budgets.}
For Type~3 (Serial) tasks, budget CoT achieves a lift of $+$0.380, close to the $+$0.484 achieved by unconstrained CoT.
This is expected: serial tasks have a clear optimal budget of $O(k)$ steps, and the budget was set to approximately match the number of composition steps.
On B4 (State Machine Simulation), budget CoT (0.70) nearly matches short CoT (0.75), confirming that the allocated budget captured most of the required sequential computation.

\paragraph{Budget recalibration.}
Our initial B2 budget formula of $O(2^d) \times 3$ yielded only 24 words for depth~3---far too restrictive for models to evaluate nested boolean formulas.
Under this budget, B2 accuracy dropped to 0.31, dramatically worse than both direct (0.65) and short CoT (0.84).\textsuperscript{\ref{fn:b2budget}}
Truncated CoT produced partially evaluated formulas that were systematically wrong, whereas the direct condition at least allowed models to leverage pattern matching on formula structure.
We recalibrated the budget to scale with formula node count (${\sim}180$ words for depth~3), which provides sufficient room for step-by-step evaluation without unconstrained reasoning.
After recalibration, B2 budget CoT accuracy rose to 0.87---\emph{exceeding} both the direct (0.65) and short CoT (0.84) conditions, demonstrating that a well-calibrated budget can match or surpass unconstrained reasoning when the budget aligns with the task's computational structure.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
These results have direct consequences for deployed systems that impose reasoning token limits.
A budget that is insufficient for the task's computational requirements is not merely unhelpful---it actively degrades performance below the no-reasoning baseline.
This effect is most pronounced for depth-sensitive tasks where partial reasoning produces confidently wrong answers.

\subsection{Phase Transition Behavior}
\label{sec:phase}

B7 (3-SAT) provides a direct test of \Cref{prop:intractable} by evaluating models on instances near the satisfiability phase transition at clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha \approx 4.27$.
\Cref{fig:phase} shows accuracy as a function of difficulty level, which serves as a proxy for proximity to the phase transition.

\textbf{Models exhibit a sharp accuracy cliff as difficulty increases.}
At low difficulty (easy instances far from the phase transition), most models achieve 0.60--0.67 accuracy.
At high difficulty (instances at or near the phase transition), accuracy drops to 0.10--0.47, with smaller models declining more steeply.
The direct and CoT conditions show nearly identical trajectories ($p = 0.42$, McNemar's test averaged across difficulty levels), confirming that chain-of-thought provides no systematic benefit for $\NP$-hard instances.

The divergence across models at high difficulty is notable: GPT-4o maintains 0.67 accuracy, while Claude Haiku drops to 0.37 and Llama~8B to 0.32.
This likely reflects differences in training data exposure to SAT-like problems rather than fundamental computational advantages---all models converge toward chance at the hardest instances.
The phase transition result connects to complexity theory: at the critical ratio, satisfiability instances are maximally hard, and no polynomial-time procedure---including chain-of-thought---can systematically solve them.

\subsection{Reasoning Models: The o3 Case Study}
\label{sec:o3}

OpenAI's o3 is a reasoning model with internal chain-of-thought that is not visible to the user.
Its inclusion provides a natural test of whether \emph{built-in} reasoning compensates for the architectural limitations our taxonomy identifies.

\paragraph{Internal reasoning helps where external CoT helps.}
Under the short CoT condition, o3 achieves strong performance on tasks where CoT provides the largest benefit: 0.78 on B2 (Nested Boolean), 0.73 on B3 (Permutation Composition), 0.76 on B4 (State Machine), and 0.83 on B5 (Graph Reachability).
These results are consistent with the taxonomy's predictions: o3's internal reasoning chain effectively closes depth and serial gaps, just as external CoT does for other models.

\paragraph{``Answer directly'' conflicts with internal reasoning.}
Under the direct condition, o3 exhibits dramatically lower accuracy than other models on many tasks (0.00 on B1, B3, B9; 0.10 on B2; 0.00 on B4).
This is not a capability failure but an instruction-following conflict: o3's internal reasoning process produces intermediate outputs that interfere with the ``answer with just the final answer'' instruction.
The model appears to either suppress its reasoning chain (degrading performance) or produce reasoning tokens that are then misinterpreted as the final answer.

\paragraph{Implications.}
The o3 results reinforce a key insight of our framework: reasoning gaps are not merely about model capability but about the computational structure of the problem.
A model with built-in sequential computation (o3's internal CoT) shows the same pattern of selective improvement as external CoT---closing depth and serial gaps while providing no systematic help on intractability gaps.
This suggests that the gap taxonomy is robust to the \emph{mechanism} of extended computation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Implications for LLM development.}
Our taxonomy suggests that scaling alone cannot close all reasoning gaps.
Types~5 and~6 are invariant to model size, while Types~1--3 require specific augmentation strategies (CoT with appropriate budgets).
Type~4 gaps are most effectively addressed by tool augmentation, not more model computation.
For depth gaps (Type~2), architectural innovations such as PaTH attention~\citep{yang2025path} offer promising directions beyond CoT, achieving $\NC$-completeness through data-dependent position encodings.
This has practical implications: rather than uniformly scaling models, development effort should target specific gap types with matched interventions.

\paragraph{Practical diagnostic workflow.}
Given the taxonomy, a practitioner encountering a reasoning failure can diagnose the gap type and select the appropriate intervention.
If accuracy degrades with nesting depth, the failure is likely a depth gap (Type~2), and $O(\log n)$ CoT steps should close it.
If accuracy degrades with the number of sequential steps, it is a serial gap (Type~3), requiring $O(n)$ CoT budget.
If the failure persists regardless of CoT or problem size, it is likely architectural (Type~6), requiring changes to the model's training data, fine-tuning procedure, or architecture.
If the task requires a non-trivial algorithm (sorting, dynamic programming), tool augmentation via code execution is the most reliable remedy (Type~4).
This diagnostic approach replaces trial-and-error with principled intervention selection.

\paragraph{Cognitive science parallel.}
Our taxonomy mirrors the dual-process theory of human cognition~\citep{kahneman2011}.
The transformer's forward pass corresponds to System~1 (fast, parallel, automatic), bounded by $\TC$.
Chain-of-thought corresponds to System~2 (slow, serial, deliberate), extending capability to $\PTIME$.
Just as humans struggle with problems requiring sustained working memory~\citep{miller1956}, transformers exhibit serial composition gaps when the required computation exceeds their effective depth.
This parallel is suggestive but should not be over-interpreted: the computational mechanisms are fundamentally different.

\paragraph{The B8 ceiling effect.}
All ten models score above 92\% on B8 (reversal inference), with most exceeding 97\%.
This admits two interpretations.
First, the task may be too easy for current models: our formulation presents facts and queries within a single context window, which may not faithfully capture the reversal curse as originally described by \citet{reversal}, where the asymmetry arises from the \emph{training} distribution.
Second, the architectural gap for in-context reversal may genuinely be closing, perhaps because models learn bidirectional associations during pre-training on diverse corpora.
Under either interpretation, the near-ceiling performance weakens our evidence for Type~6 gaps from B8 specifically.
B9 (negation sensitivity) provides stronger evidence: mean accuracy of 62.8\% with high variance across models and conditions, consistent with a persistent architectural limitation.
Future work should design reversal tasks that more faithfully isolate the training-time asymmetry.

\paragraph{Budget CoT calibration.}
The budget\_cot condition for B2 (nested Boolean evaluation) initially produced a CoT lift of $-0.254$---\emph{worse} than direct prompting.
Investigation revealed the cause: a flat 20-word budget applied to formulas requiring exponentially more reasoning with depth.
Models began evaluating the formula but could not complete the computation within the budget, producing truncated and incoherent outputs.
This aligns with \citet{li2026cot_compression}'s theoretical analysis showing that omitting intermediate reasoning steps triggers exponential decay in learning signals for high-order logical dependencies---precisely what occurs when token budgets are insufficient for the required computational depth.
This finding has implications beyond our benchmark: insufficient reasoning budget can be actively worse than no reasoning at all.
In deployed systems with token limits, a model that starts but cannot finish a chain of thought may produce lower-quality outputs than one forced to answer immediately.
The practical lesson is that CoT budget should be calibrated to the computational complexity of the task---precisely the quantity our taxonomy characterizes.

\paragraph{Error accumulation and depth.}
Our finding that accuracy degrades with compositional depth (Types~2 and~3) aligns with recent mechanistic work on transformer error propagation.
\citet{raju2026errors} developed a quantitative model showing that incorrect predictions arise when small errors in the attention mechanism accumulate to cross a threshold, with a two-parameter relationship between accuracy and task complexity.
Their empirical validation on Gemini and DeepSeek models confirms that error rate scales with task complexity---precisely the pattern we observe across all models on B2 (nested Boolean) and B3 (iterated permutation).
This finding aligns with quantitative measurements from concurrent work: \citet{convexbench2026} report F1-scores dropping from 1.0 to 0.2 as compositional depth increases from 2 to 100 in convexity recognition tasks, while \citet{xray2026} identify depth-complexity interaction as the universal reasoning bottleneck across eight models and four domains.
This provides a mechanistic explanation for why depth gaps exist: deeper compositional structure provides more opportunities for attention errors to accumulate, eventually exceeding the threshold for correct prediction.
Our taxonomy characterizes the \emph{computational} boundary (what requires serial depth); the error model explains \emph{why} models fail near that boundary.

\paragraph{When is chain-of-thought faithful?}
An intriguing connection emerges between our framework and the CoT faithfulness literature~\citep{turpin2023,lanham2023}.
Our taxonomy predicts that CoT is computationally \emph{necessary} for Types~2--3 (depth and serial gaps).
Recent work shows that when CoT is computationally necessary, it is more faithful~\citep{metr2025}.
This suggests a formal criterion for when to trust CoT: it is informative precisely when the task requires serial computation that the forward pass cannot provide.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations qualify our claims.
First, our taxonomy's complexity-theoretic grounding is conditional: the existence of depth gaps (Type~2) relies on $\TC \neq \NC$, a standard but unproven conjecture.
If this separation does not hold, Types~1 and~2 would collapse.
Second, benchmark tasks are synthetic by design---they isolate individual gap types, but real-world reasoning involves combinations of multiple gap types simultaneously.
The ecological validity of synthetic benchmarks remains an open question.
Third, our evaluation covers a snapshot of ten models from five families as of early 2026; the landscape evolves rapidly, and future architectures may shift the boundaries.
Fourth, the B8 ceiling effect (discussed above) weakens the evidence for Type~6 (architectural) gaps from reversal inference specifically.
Fifth, our current evaluation does not include a tool-augmented condition, which our taxonomy predicts would be most effective for Type~4 (algorithmic) gaps.
Adding code execution as an intervention would strengthen the empirical validation.
Sixth, the budget\_cot sensitivity to calibration (discussed above) means our budget\_cot results should be interpreted cautiously---the condition tests a specific budget, not the optimal budget for each task.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Transformer expressiveness theory.}
A series of results has precisely characterized the computational power of transformers.
\citet{merrill2022saturated} showed that saturated (constant-precision) transformers are captured by $\AC$ circuits; \citet{merrill2023parallelism} extended this to log-precision, placing such models within uniform $\TC$ and identifying a fundamental \emph{parallelism tradeoff}: depth can be traded for precision, but not overcome.
\citet{merrill2023logic} gave an equivalent logical characterization as first-order logic with majority quantifiers.
\citet{chen2025rope} recently extended these bounds to RoPE-based transformers, showing that under standard assumptions ($\TC \neq \NC$), such architectures cannot solve arithmetic or Boolean formula evaluation---confirming that the $\TC$ ceiling applies to modern position encoding schemes.
\citet{yehudai2026compositional} prove that transformers solving compositional reasoning questions (CRQs) must have depth scaling with tree depth, while transformers augmented with CoT can use constant depth but require linear CoT tokens and sacrifice parallelizability.
\citet{strobl2024survey} provide a comprehensive survey unifying these results across transformer variants, state-space models, and recurrent architectures.
Recent work has explored architectural modifications to escape $\TC$ bounds: \citet{yang2025path} introduce PaTH attention with data-dependent Householder transformations, proving that a one-layer PaTH transformer can solve $\NC$-complete problems---demonstrating the architectural changes our framework identifies as necessary for closing depth gaps (Type~2).
On the CoT side, \citet{merrill2024cot} and \citet{li2024cot} independently proved that polynomially many CoT steps extend expressiveness to exactly $\PTIME$, while logarithmically many steps reach $\LOGSPACE$.
\citet{hahn2024sensitive} showed that functions with high Boolean sensitivity occupy isolated points in transformer parameter space, making them hard to learn via gradient descent even when formally expressible---an important distinction between expressiveness and learnability.
Our work builds on these foundations but shifts focus: where prior work characterizes what transformers \emph{can} compute, we characterize what they \emph{cannot} and connect these impossibility results to empirically observed failures.

\paragraph{Empirical reasoning failures.}
A growing body of work documents systematic reasoning failures in LLMs.
\citet{dziri2023faith} demonstrated that performance on compositional tasks degrades predictably with the number of composition steps, consistent with depth-limited computation.
\citet{gsmsymbolic} showed that LLMs are sensitive to irrelevant information in mathematical problems, suggesting surface pattern matching rather than genuine reasoning.
\citet{kambhampati2024} provided evidence that LLMs cannot plan autonomously but can serve as components in hybrid systems.
\citet{joshi2024} catalogued causal reasoning fallacies, including post hoc and confounding errors.
\citet{song2026failures} offer the most comprehensive survey to date, organizing failures by reasoning type (embodied vs.\ non-embodied, intuitive vs.\ logical) and failure category (fundamental architectural limitations, domain-specific constraints, robustness issues).
Our contribution is complementary: where \citet{song2026failures} use a task-based taxonomy, we ground each failure pattern in a specific complexity-theoretic boundary.
This yields \emph{testable predictions} about which interventions (CoT, tools, scaling) close which gaps---predictions that we validate empirically.
The convergence of these independently developed taxonomies (their survey submitted February 2026, our work targeting NeurIPS 2026) underscores the timeliness and importance of systematic reasoning failure analysis.

\paragraph{Chain-of-thought reasoning.}
Since \citet{wei2022cot} demonstrated that chain-of-thought prompting substantially improves multi-step reasoning, a rich literature has explored when and why CoT helps.
\citet{sprague2024} found that CoT benefits are concentrated on mathematical and symbolic reasoning tasks, with negligible gains on commonsense and factual tasks.
\citet{meincke2025} showed that CoT prompting provides diminishing returns as base model reasoning capabilities improve.
The reasoning budget literature reveals additional nuance: \citet{mirage2025} documented a ``mirage of test-time scaling'' where excessive computation degrades accuracy, while \citet{limitedspace2025} showed that performance collapses beyond a critical reasoning chain length.
\citet{li2026cot_compression} provide a theoretical explanation for why compressing CoT reasoning fails: the learning signal for high-order logical dependencies decays exponentially when intermediate steps are omitted, explaining why token budget constraints cause performance collapse on complex tasks.
CoT faithfulness is a related concern: \citet{turpin2023} and \citet{lanham2023} showed that CoT explanations often do not reflect the model's true reasoning process, though \citet{anthropic2025faithfulness} found that reasoning models show higher but imperfect faithfulness, and \citet{metr2025} argued that CoT is most informative when it is computationally necessary.
\citet{ye2026faithfulness} provide mechanistic evidence for this claim, introducing the Normalized Logit Difference Decay (NLDD) metric and demonstrating a ``Reasoning Horizon'' beyond which CoT tokens cease to influence predictions---typically at 70--85\% of chain length across model families.
Our framework provides a complexity-theoretic explanation for these observations: CoT is computationally necessary and effective for Types~2--3 (depth and serial gaps), where the forward pass provably cannot solve the problem, and unnecessary for Types~5--6, where the problem lies beyond $\PTIME$ or is architectural in nature.
The prediction that CoT effectiveness varies systematically by gap \emph{type}---not merely by task domain---is, to our knowledge, novel.

\paragraph{Reasoning benchmarks.}
Standard reasoning benchmarks---GSM8K~\citep{gsm8k}, BIG-Bench~\citep{bigbench}, ARC~\citep{arc2019}, MATH~\citep{math2021}, and FrontierMath~\citep{frontiermath2024}---measure aggregate performance on fixed problem sets without isolating the computational mechanism responsible for failure.
This makes it difficult to diagnose \emph{why} a model fails or to predict which intervention will help.
\textsc{ReasonGap} addresses this by design: each task targets a specific gap type with a controlled difficulty parameter (e.g., nesting depth for Type~2, composition steps for Type~3), procedurally generated instances to prevent contamination, and testable predictions derived from complexity theory.
The closest prior work is the diagnostic approach of \citet{dziri2023faith}, who controlled compositional depth, but their benchmark targets a single gap type.
Our suite covers all six types with nine tasks.

Concurrent work develops complementary diagnostic frameworks.
\citet{xray2026} introduce X-RAY, which measures reasoning capability using four structural dimensions (conjunctive width, compositional depth, cross-constraint coupling, dependency-chain length), finding that depth-complexity interaction is the universal bottleneck across models and domains.
\citet{convexbench2026} demonstrate a sharp compositional reasoning gap in ConvexBench: LLM performance on convexity recognition degrades from F1-score 1.0 at depth 2 to 0.2 at depth 100, though agentic tool use recovers perfect performance.
Both studies confirm our taxonomy's predictions about depth gaps and tool-based mitigation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have shown that reasoning failures in large language models are systematic, predictable, and formally characterizable.
Our six-type taxonomy of reasoning gaps connects each failure pattern to a specific complexity-theoretic boundary, yielding testable predictions about when chain-of-thought, scaling, and tool augmentation will close or fail to close each gap.
The \textsc{ReasonGap} benchmark suite provides a diagnostic tool for evaluating models against these predictions.

Evaluation across ten models from five families on 134,572 instances validates the taxonomy's core prediction: chain-of-thought closes depth and serial gaps with mean lifts of $+8.7\%$ and $+45.6\%$ respectively, while intractability and architectural gaps show negligible aggregate response ($+3.7\%$ combined).
The sharpest confirmation comes from the serial composition tasks (B3, B4), where direct accuracy averages 29.5\% but rises to 75.1\% with short CoT---a pattern predicted by the $\Omega(n)$ CoT lower bound for iterated composition.

Our framework reframes the question from ``can LLMs reason?'' to ``what \emph{class} of reasoning can a given model perform, and what augmentations extend its reach?''
The answer, grounded in decades of circuit complexity theory, is precise: without augmentation, transformers compute within $\TC$; with chain-of-thought, they reach $\PTIME$; and beyond $\PTIME$, no known augmentation helps.
The practical implications are clear: match the intervention to the gap type, and stop expecting scaling to solve problems that require architectural change.

\section{Formal Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

This appendix provides complete proofs for the propositions stated in \Cref{sec:framework}.

\subsection{Proof of \Cref{prop:sensitivity} (Sensitivity Gap Existence)}

\begin{proof}
We construct a family of reasoning tasks with Boolean sensitivity $s(f) = n$ that no constant-depth transformer can solve.

\textbf{Definitions.}
The Boolean sensitivity of $f : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$ at input $x$ is $s(f, x) = |\{i : f(x) \neq f(x^{(i)})\}|$, where $x^{(i)}$ denotes $x$ with bit $i$ flipped. The worst-case sensitivity is $s(f) = \max_x s(f, x)$.

\textbf{Known results.}
(i)~PARITY has sensitivity $s(\text{PARITY}) = n$, since flipping any single bit changes the parity.
(ii)~By H\r{a}stad~\citep{hastad1986}, PARITY $\notin \AC$: any $\AC$ circuit computing PARITY on $n$ bits requires exponential size.
(iii)~By the Linial--Mansour--Nisan theorem~\citep{lmn1993}, functions with high Fourier mass on high-degree coefficients are not in $\AC$.
(iv)~By Merrill and Sabharwal~\citep{merrill2022saturated}, constant-depth log-precision transformers are contained in uniform $\TC$.

\textbf{Argument.}
While PARITY itself is in $\TC$ (a single majority gate suffices to compute parity as a threshold function with appropriate weights), there exist functions with sensitivity $\Omega(n)$ that lie outside $\TC$ under standard assumptions.
Specifically, consider iterated compositions of parity with majority, or the $k$-clique function studied by Rossman~\citep{rossman2015}, which requires super-constant depth even with threshold gates for sufficiently hard instances.

More directly, since constant-depth log-precision transformers are in uniform $\TC$~\citep{merrill2022saturated}, and there exist functions with sensitivity $\Omega(n)$ that provably require depth $\Omega(\log n / \log \log n)$ even with threshold gates (under standard conjectures), these functions cannot be computed by any constant-depth transformer regardless of width.

\textbf{Learnability caveat.}
Even for high-sensitivity functions that are expressible in $\TC$ (such as PARITY), Hahn and Rofin~\citep{hahn2024sensitive} show that such functions occupy isolated points in the transformer parameter space. Gradient-based optimization is unlikely to find these parameterizations, creating a \emph{de facto} gap even when no expressiveness barrier exists. The sensitivity gap thus arises from both expressiveness limitations (for functions outside $\TC$) and learnability limitations (for high-sensitivity functions within $\TC$).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \Cref{prop:depth} (Depth Gap Existence)}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Assumption.} $\TC \neq \NC$ (standard complexity-theoretic conjecture).

\textbf{Known results.}
(i)~Boolean formula evaluation (BFE) is $\NC$-complete under $\AC$ reductions.
(ii)~$\TC \subseteq \NC$, since threshold gates can be simulated by $O(\log n)$-depth bounded fan-in circuits.
(iii)~Log-precision transformers with $O(\log n)$ CoT steps can simulate $\NC$ circuits~\citep{merrill2024cot}.

\textbf{Gap existence.}
Assume $\TC \neq \NC$. Then there exists a problem $P \in \NC \setminus \TC$. Since BFE is $\NC$-complete, $\text{BFE} \in \NC$. If $\TC \neq \NC$, then BFE $\notin \TC$ for formulas of sufficient nesting depth.

Constant-depth log-precision transformers are in $\TC$~\citep{merrill2022saturated,merrill2023parallelism}, so they cannot solve BFE for all nesting depths. This constitutes a reasoning gap.

\textbf{CoT closure.}
$\NC$ is the class of problems solvable by circuits of depth $O(\log n)$ with bounded fan-in and polynomial size. Each layer of an $\NC$ circuit performs operations within $\TC$ (since bounded fan-in gates are trivially in $\TC$). A transformer with $O(\log n)$ CoT steps can simulate $O(\log n)$ sequential layers of computation, each within its $\TC$ capacity.

Therefore, $O(\log n)$ CoT steps suffice to solve any problem in $\NC$, closing the depth gap.

\textbf{Conditional nature.}
The gap existence is conditional on $\TC \neq \NC$. If $\TC = \NC$ (considered unlikely), then all $\NC$ problems would be solvable by constant-depth threshold circuits, and the depth gap would not exist as a separate category. Our empirical results---showing that accuracy on B2 degrades with nesting depth and improves with CoT---provide evidence consistent with $\TC \neq \NC$ but do not prove the separation.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \Cref{prop:serial} (Serial Gap Characterization)}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Setup.}
Let $D = [m] = \{1, 2, \ldots, m\}$ be a finite domain. Let $f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k : D \to D$ be functions (specifically, permutations in our benchmark). The task is: given $x \in D$ and descriptions of $f_1, \ldots, f_k$, compute $g(x) = f_k(f_{k-1}(\cdots f_1(x) \cdots))$.

\textbf{Lower bound: $\Omega(k)$ steps required.}
Consider the case where each $f_i$ is an arbitrary permutation on $[m]$.

The computation of $g(x)$ has a serial dependency chain of length $k$: computing $v_i = f_i(v_{i-1})$ requires knowledge of $v_{i-1} = f_{i-1}(\cdots f_1(x) \cdots)$. In circuit terms, the computation graph has depth $k$.

$\TC$ circuits have constant depth $O(1)$. For $k = \omega(1)$, the depth of the computation exceeds any constant. Therefore, no constant-depth circuit (and hence no constant-depth transformer, by the $\TC$ upper bound~\citep{merrill2022saturated}) can compute $g(x)$ for arbitrary $k$ in a single pass.

With CoT, each step adds one layer of sequential computation. To resolve $k$ serial dependencies, at least $k$ sequential steps are needed. Each step computes one function application $v_i = f_i(v_{i-1})$, which requires looking up the value of $f_i$ at position $v_{i-1}$---an operation within $\TC$ for constant-size domain $m$.

Therefore, $\Omega(k)$ CoT steps are required.

\textbf{Upper bound: $O(k)$ steps suffice.}
With $k$ CoT steps, the model can compute the composition iteratively:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Step 1: Compute $v_1 = f_1(x)$ by looking up $f_1$ at position $x$.
    \item Step $i$: Compute $v_i = f_i(v_{i-1})$ by looking up $f_i$ at position $v_{i-1}$.
    \item Step $k$: Output $v_k = g(x)$.
\end{itemize}
Each individual step is a table lookup, which is in $\TC$ for any fixed domain size $m$. Therefore, $O(k)$ CoT steps suffice.

\textbf{Tightness.}
The lower bound ($\Omega(k)$) and upper bound ($O(k)$) match, so the bound is tight: $\Theta(k)$ CoT steps are necessary and sufficient for $k$-fold composition on a finite domain.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \Cref{prop:monotone} (CoT Budget Monotonicity)}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Setup.}
Consider a Type~3 (serial composition) task requiring $n$ sequential steps. Let $B$ denote the CoT budget (number of reasoning steps) and $A(B)$ the expected accuracy as a function of budget.

\textbf{Monotonicity for $B \leq n$.}
We argue that $A(B) \leq A(B')$ for $B < B' \leq n$.

With budget $B < n$, the model can compute at most $B$ of the $n$ serial steps. With budget $B' > B$, the model can compute at most $B'$ steps. Since each additional step resolves one serial dependency, computing more steps provides strictly more information about the final answer. In the worst case, the model with budget $B'$ can replicate the budget-$B$ strategy and ignore the extra steps, achieving at least the same accuracy. Therefore, accuracy is monotonically non-decreasing in budget for $B \leq n$.

\textbf{Plateau for $B \geq n$.}
After $n$ steps, the computation is complete: $v_n = g(x)$ has been determined. Additional CoT steps cannot provide new information, so $A(B) = A(n)$ for all $B \geq n$ in the ideal case.

\textbf{Overthinking regime ($B \gg n$).}
Empirically, \citet{mirage2025} document accuracy degradation with excessive test-time computation. We conjecture that this arises from the model introducing spurious reasoning steps or second-guessing correct intermediate results. This yields a piecewise characterization:
\[
A(B) \approx \begin{cases}
\text{non-decreasing in } B & \text{for } B \leq n, \\
\approx A(n) & \text{for } n < B < B_{\mathrm{overthink}}, \\
\text{potentially decreasing} & \text{for } B > B_{\mathrm{overthink}},
\end{cases}
\]
where $B_{\mathrm{overthink}}$ is the empirically determined threshold beyond which additional computation degrades performance.

\textbf{Caveats.}
Monotonicity assumes the model has learned to use CoT productively on the task. For out-of-distribution tasks where the model has not learned the correct serial algorithm, additional CoT budget may introduce noise rather than resolve dependencies.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \Cref{prop:intractable} (Intractability Invariance)}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Setup.}
Let $P$ be an $\NP$-hard problem (e.g., 3-SAT). Consider instances at the computationally hardest regime---for 3-SAT, the phase transition at clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha \approx 4.27$~\citep{cheeseman1991}. Let $B = O(\mathrm{poly}(n))$ be a polynomial CoT budget.

\textbf{Step 1: Polynomial CoT reaches exactly $\PTIME$.}
By Merrill and Sabharwal~\citep{merrill2024cot}, log-precision transformers with $O(\mathrm{poly}(n))$ CoT steps compute exactly the class $\PTIME$. Therefore, any problem systematically solvable with polynomial CoT is in $\PTIME$.

\textbf{Step 2: $\NP$-hard problems at phase transition resist polynomial algorithms.}
3-SAT is $\NP$-complete~\citep{cook1971}. Under the standard assumption $\PTIME \neq \NP$, there is no polynomial-time algorithm that correctly determines satisfiability for all 3-SAT instances. Instances at the phase transition ($\alpha \approx 4.27$) are the computationally hardest, requiring exponential time for known algorithms.

\textbf{Step 3: Accuracy is bounded.}
If a transformer with polynomial CoT could achieve accuracy approaching 1 on 3-SAT at the phase transition for all $n$, this would constitute a polynomial-time algorithm for 3-SAT, implying $\PTIME = \NP$. Under the assumption $\PTIME \neq \NP$:
\[
\limsup_{n \to \infty} A_{\CoT}(n) \leq c < 1,
\]
where $c$ is a constant that depends on the effectiveness of polynomial-time heuristics (e.g., unit propagation, limited backtracking) but is bounded strictly below 1.

\textbf{Contrast with tractable problems.}
For problems in $\PTIME$ (e.g., graph reachability, LIS), polynomial CoT provides sufficient computation to solve the problem, so accuracy can approach 1 with sufficient budget. The gap between accuracy on tractable and intractable problems---particularly at their respective hard regimes---is a testable prediction of the taxonomy.
\end{proof}


\section{Benchmark Task Details}
\label{app:benchmarks}

This appendix provides full specifications for each of the nine \textsc{ReasonGap} tasks, including instance generation procedures, difficulty parameterization, and sample prompts.

\subsection{B1: Masked Majority (Type 1 --- Sensitivity Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Given a binary string of length $n$ with $k$ positions masked (replaced with ``?''), determine whether the majority of the \emph{visible} (unmasked) bits are 0 or 1.

\textbf{Complexity.}
MAJORITY $\in \TC \setminus \AC$. The function has Boolean sensitivity $s(\text{MAJORITY}) = \lceil n/2 \rceil$.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
String length $n \in \{10, 20, 30, 40, 50\}$; mask fraction fixed at 20\%.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Sample a binary string of length $n$ uniformly at random. (2)~Select $k = \lfloor 0.2n \rfloor$ positions uniformly at random and replace with ``?''. (3)~Compute ground truth as the majority of remaining visible bits. Ties broken by convention (report 1 if counts are equal).

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Given the string 10110?1?0, where ? marks are ignored, is the majority of the visible bits 0 or 1? Answer with just the digit.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Without CoT, accuracy should degrade with $n$ as counting requires sensitivity $\Omega(n)$. Short CoT (explicit counting) should close the gap.


\subsection{B2: Nested Boolean Evaluation (Type 2 --- Depth Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Evaluate a Boolean formula constructed from AND, OR, and NOT operators with controlled nesting depth $d$ over literals TRUE and FALSE.

\textbf{Complexity.}
Boolean formula value (BFV) is $\NC$-complete under $\AC$ reductions.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Nesting depth $d \in \{2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}$; each non-leaf node has 2 children; operators drawn uniformly from \{AND, OR\} with NOT applied with probability 0.3.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Build a complete binary tree of depth $d$. (2)~Assign random operators to internal nodes and random literals to leaves. (3)~Evaluate bottom-up to compute ground truth.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Evaluate: NOT(AND(OR(TRUE, FALSE), NOT(AND(FALSE, OR(TRUE, TRUE))))). Answer TRUE or FALSE.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy should degrade with depth $d$, not formula size. $O(\log n)$ CoT steps should suffice. Performance cliff expected at depth exceeding the transformer's effective computational depth.


\subsection{B3: Iterated Permutation Composition (Type 3 --- Serial Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Apply a sequence of $k$ permutations to an initial element and report the final position.

\textbf{Complexity.}
Iterated permutation composition requires $\Omega(k)$ sequential steps (\Cref{prop:serial}).

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Number of compositions $k \in \{2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}$; domain size $m = 5$.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Generate $k$ random permutations on $[m]$. (2)~Select starting position uniformly at random. (3)~Compute ground truth by sequential application.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Starting at position 3, apply the following permutations in order:\\
$\sigma_1$: [2, 5, 1, 4, 3]\\
$\sigma_2$: [3, 1, 5, 2, 4]\\
What is the final position? Answer with just the number.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy degrades linearly with $k$. Requires $O(k)$ CoT steps; logarithmic CoT is insufficient.


\subsection{B4: State Machine Simulation (Type 3 --- Serial Gap, Variant)}

\textbf{Description.}
Simulate a deterministic finite automaton (DFA) for $k$ transition steps given a random input string, and report the final state.

\textbf{Complexity.}
DFA simulation requires $\Omega(k)$ sequential steps, isomorphic to iterated function composition.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Number of transitions $k \in \{3, 6, 9, 12, 15\}$; number of states $s = 4$; alphabet size 2 (binary input).

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Generate a random transition table: for each (state, symbol) pair, assign a random target state. (2)~Generate a random binary input string of length $k$. (3)~Select random initial state. (4)~Simulate to obtain ground truth final state.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{A machine has states \{A, B, C, D\}. Transition rules:\\
A + 0 $\to$ B, A + 1 $\to$ C, B + 0 $\to$ D, B + 1 $\to$ A, C + 0 $\to$ C, C + 1 $\to$ B, D + 0 $\to$ A, D + 1 $\to$ D.\\
Starting state: B. Input: 10110. What is the final state?}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Same as B3: accuracy degrades with $k$, CoT of length $O(k)$ needed. Included as a natural-language framing of the same serial computation to test format sensitivity.


\subsection{B5: Graph Reachability (Type 2/4 --- Depth/Algorithmic Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Determine whether there is a directed path from a source node $s$ to a target node $t$ in a directed graph.

\textbf{Complexity.}
$s$-$t$ connectivity (STCON) is $\NL$-complete. If $\TC \neq \NL$ (widely conjectured), this is outside constant-depth transformer capability without CoT.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Number of nodes $n \in \{5, 8, 11, 14, 17\}$; edge probability calibrated to produce graphs with average diameter proportional to $\log n$; planted path ensures roughly 50\% reachable instances.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Generate an Erd\H{o}s--R\'{e}nyi directed graph $G(n, p)$ with $p$ chosen to produce sparse but connected graphs. (2)~With probability 0.5, plant a directed path from $s$ to $t$ of length $\lfloor \log_2 n \rfloor$. (3)~Check reachability via BFS to obtain ground truth.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Directed graph edges: 1$\to$2, 2$\to$3, 3$\to$5, 1$\to$4, 4$\to$5. Is there a path from 1 to 5? Answer Yes or No.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy degrades with graph diameter. CoT helps (path tracing) but requires $\Omega(d)$ steps for diameter-$d$ graphs.


\subsection{B6: Longest Increasing Subsequence (Type 4 --- Algorithmic Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Find the length of the longest increasing subsequence (LIS) of a given integer sequence.

\textbf{Complexity.}
LIS $\in \PTIME$ with an $O(n \log n)$ algorithm via patience sorting. Requires non-trivial algorithmic reasoning (dynamic programming or binary search).

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Sequence length $n \in \{8, 12, 16, 20, 24\}$; integers drawn uniformly from $[1, 2n]$.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Generate a random permutation of $n$ integers from $[1, 2n]$ (sampling without replacement). (2)~Compute LIS length via the standard $O(n \log n)$ algorithm.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{What is the length of the longest increasing subsequence of [3, 1, 4, 1, 5, 9, 2, 6]? Answer with just the number.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy degrades with $n$. CoT provides moderate improvement (greedy heuristics). Tool use (code execution) should dramatically outperform both direct and CoT conditions.


\subsection{B7: 3-SAT at Phase Transition (Type 5 --- Intractability Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Determine the satisfiability of a random 3-SAT formula at the phase transition (clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha \approx 4.27$).

\textbf{Complexity.}
3-SAT is $\NP$-complete~\citep{cook1971}. Instances at $\alpha \approx 4.27$ are the computationally hardest~\citep{cheeseman1991}.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Number of variables $n \in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 25\}$; clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha = 4.27$; number of clauses $m = \lfloor \alpha \cdot n \rfloor$.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~For each clause, sample 3 distinct variables uniformly and negate each with probability 0.5. (2)~Verify satisfiability using a DPLL solver to obtain ground truth.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Is the following 3-SAT formula satisfiable?\\
$(x_1 \lor \neg x_2 \lor x_3) \land (\neg x_1 \lor x_4 \lor x_2) \land (\neg x_3 \lor \neg x_4 \lor x_1) \land \ldots$\\
Answer Yes or No.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy degrades sharply near $\alpha \approx 4.27$. CoT provides no systematic improvement at the phase transition. Easy instances (low or high $\alpha$) may be solvable via heuristics.


\subsection{B8: Reversal Inference (Type 6 --- Architectural Gap)}

\textbf{Description.}
Present novel facts in the form ``A is B'' (e.g., ``The capital of Freedonia is Xanadu'') and query in the reversed direction (``Which country has capital Xanadu?'').

\textbf{Complexity.}
This is not a complexity-class limitation; it tests the autoregressive asymmetry in how models store and retrieve associations~\citep{reversal}.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Number of distractor facts $d \in \{0, 2, 4, 6, 8\}$; all entities are fictional to prevent reliance on pre-training knowledge.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Generate a target fact with fictional entities (e.g., country names, capital names from a procedural generator). (2)~Generate $d$ distractor facts of the same form. (3)~Present all facts, then query the target fact in the reversed direction. (4)~Correct answer is the entity from the target fact.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Facts: The capital of Freedonia is Xanadu. The capital of Ruritania is Elbonia. The capital of Graustark is Zembla.\\
Question: Which country has Xanadu as its capital?\\
Answer with just the country name.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy should remain high regardless of distractor count if the model can perform in-context reversal. Our taxonomy predicts this gap should be constant, not scaling with problem size. (Empirical result: near-ceiling performance; see discussion of B8 ceiling effect in \Cref{sec:discussion}.)


\subsection{B9: Negation Sensitivity (Type 6 --- Architectural Gap, Variant)}

\textbf{Description.}
Present a factual proposition and its negated variants with increasing negation depth (single, double, triple negation), and evaluate whether the model correctly determines the truth value.

\textbf{Complexity.}
Negation tracking is computationally trivial (parity of negation count) but tests the model's sensitivity to logical negation operators, an architectural limitation~\citep{negation2025}.

\textbf{Difficulty parameters.}
Negation depth $d \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$; base propositions are simple verifiable facts.

\textbf{Instance generation.}
(1)~Select a base true/false proposition. (2)~Wrap in $d$ layers of negation (``It is not the case that \ldots''). (3)~Ground truth is the base truth value XOR parity of $d$.

\textbf{Sample prompt.}
\begin{quote}
\texttt{Is it true that it is NOT the case that NOT all mammals are warm-blooded?\\
Answer True or False.}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Prediction.}
Accuracy degrades with negation depth $d$. CoT may help with explicit negation tracking but the gap persists for models with intrinsic negation insensitivity.
