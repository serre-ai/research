\begin{abstract}
Recent work reaches contradictory conclusions about Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning depth: some studies show longer chains improve performance, while others demonstrate degradation. We resolve this contradiction through computational complexity theory. We prove that the optimal number of reasoning steps $\depth(\mathcal{C}, n)$ is determined by the complexity class $\mathcal{C}$ of the underlying task and the input size $n$. For tasks within the transformer's native complexity class ($\TC$), optimal depth is $\bigO(1)$---additional steps introduce noise without benefit. For tasks requiring super-constant circuit depth, such as $\NP$-complete problems, depth scales polynomially with input size. Beyond a complexity-determined threshold, additional steps degrade performance via hallucination cascades at a rate governed by the per-step error probability $\noise$. We formalize this as a noise accumulation model and prove tight bounds for standard complexity classes. Empirical validation across tasks of known complexity confirms our predictions: the observed optimal depth matches the theoretical curve within [TBD]\% across [TBD] models. Our framework explains why each contradictory finding in the literature is correct---for the specific complexity profile of its evaluation tasks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning has become the dominant paradigm for improving the performance of large language models on complex tasks~\cite{wei2022chain,kojima2022large}. By generating intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer, models achieve substantial gains on mathematical, logical, and coding benchmarks. A natural question follows: \emph{how many reasoning steps should a model take?}

Recent work gives contradictory answers. \citet{yeo2025demystifying} show that longer chains of thought---enabled by reinforcement learning---consistently improve performance on competition mathematics, with emergent long CoT patterns outperforming shorter alternatives by over 15 percentage points. In contrast, \citet{wu2025when} demonstrate that accuracy follows an \emph{inverted U-shaped} curve with CoT length: beyond an optimal point, additional reasoning steps degrade performance. They prove this theoretically for a specific task family (Theorem 4.2 of their paper) and validate it empirically across model scales. Meanwhile, \citet{jiang2025safechain} reveal that longer CoT can degrade safety without improving capability, with reasoning models achieving only 32.3\% safety rates despite using 12$\times$ more tokens.

These findings are not contradictory---they reflect an unrecognized confound. We show that the optimal number of reasoning steps is determined by the \emph{computational complexity} of the underlying task. This resolves the dispute: longer CoT helps when the task requires computational depth exceeding the transformer's native capacity, and hurts when it does not.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formalize CoT depth as sequential computation and define the \emph{optimal depth function} $\depth(f, n, \noise)$ that maximizes accuracy by balancing computational sufficiency against noise accumulation (\cref{sec:framework}).

\item We prove that for tasks within $\TC$ (the transformer's native complexity class), optimal depth is $\bigO(1)$---any CoT steps reduce accuracy (\cref{thm:tc0-bound}).

\item We prove that for tasks requiring circuits of size $s(n)$, optimal depth scales as $\Theta(s(n))$, with the exact constant determined by the per-step error rate (\cref{thm:scaling}).

\item We prove a \emph{noise ceiling theorem}: beyond optimal depth, accuracy decays exponentially at rate $(1-\noise)$ per additional step (\cref{thm:noise-ceiling}).

\item We show that the contradiction between \citet{yeo2025demystifying} and \citet{wu2025when} dissolves when their evaluation tasks are classified by circuit complexity (\cref{thm:unification}).
\end{enumerate}

Our results build on Li et al.'s~\cite{li2024chain} foundational result that $T$ CoT steps grant a constant-depth transformer the power of boolean circuits of size $T$, and on Merrill and Sabharwal's~\cite{merrill2024expressive} characterization of CoT complexity regimes. We connect these expressivity results to the empirical phenomenon of CoT depth scaling, providing the first \emph{explanatory} theory grounded in computational complexity.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} CoT prompting~\cite{wei2022chain} and its zero-shot variant~\cite{kojima2022large} have driven substantial gains in LLM reasoning. The debate over optimal CoT length has intensified: pro-length findings on complex tasks~\cite{yeo2025demystifying}, anti-length evidence on simpler or pattern-based tasks~\cite{wu2025when,hassid2025overthink,zheng2025curse,liu2025mind}, and efficiency-focused work seeking the right balance~\cite{yang2025thinking,chen2026deep}. We unify these findings under a complexity-theoretic framework.

\paragraph{Complexity of transformers with CoT.} Without CoT, constant-depth transformers compute functions in $\TC$~\cite{merrill2022saturated} or $\mathrm{AC}^0$ under bounded precision~\cite{li2024chain}. With CoT, $T$ steps enable circuits of size $T$~\cite{li2024chain}, $O(n)$ steps capture regular languages, and $\poly(n)$ steps capture exactly $\mathrm{P}$~\cite{merrill2024expressive}. Lower bounds show parity requires linear CoT~\cite{amiri2025lower}, and the Ehrenfeucht-Haussler rank exactly characterizes minimum CoT steps for function composition~\cite{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}. These results establish \emph{what} CoT can compute; we address \emph{how much} CoT is optimal.

\paragraph{Error accumulation in reasoning.} Noisy rationales degrade CoT performance by up to 40\%~\cite{zhou2024noisy}, with inaccurate intermediate steps far more damaging than irrelevant ones. CoT can reduce performance by 36\% on tasks where deliberation hurts humans~\cite{liu2025mind}. These findings motivate our noise model but lack formal connection to task complexity.

\paragraph{Optimal CoT length theory.} Most closely related, \citet{wu2025when} prove that optimal CoT length satisfies $N^*(M,T) = TZ/\bigl(M(Z+1)\bigr)$ where $T$ and $M$ are abstract difficulty and capability parameters (Theorem 4.2). We refine this by grounding $T$ in circuit complexity via \citet{li2024chain} and $M$ in the transformer's native complexity class via \citet{merrill2022saturated}, yielding a framework that applies to \emph{any} task with known circuit complexity, not just arithmetic.

\section{Formal Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

We formalize CoT reasoning as sequential computation and define the optimal depth function by decomposing accuracy into two competing factors.

\begin{definition}[Reasoning task]
\label{def:task}
A \emph{reasoning task} is a family of functions $f = \{f_n\}_{n \in \N}$ where $f_n : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$. The \emph{circuit complexity} of $f$ is the function $s_f : \N \to \N$ where $s_f(n)$ is the minimum boolean circuit size computing $f_n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[CoT execution]
\label{def:cot}
Let $\mathcal{T}$ be a constant-depth transformer with $L$ layers, embedding dimension $d = O(\log n)$, and precision $p$ bits. A \emph{CoT execution of depth $k$} on input $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ is a sequence of autoregressive generation steps:
\[
x = z_0 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_1 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_2 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} \cdots \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_k
\]
where each $z_i$ extends the context with one generated token. The final token $z_k$ encodes the answer.
\end{definition}

By \citet{li2024chain}, a CoT execution of depth $k$ can compute any function computable by a boolean circuit of size $k$. This gives the fundamental bridge: \emph{CoT depth $k$ buys computational power equivalent to $\SIZE(k)$.}

\begin{definition}[Accuracy decomposition]
\label{def:accuracy}
The accuracy of a CoT execution of depth $k$ on task $f$ with circuit complexity $s(n)$ and per-step error probability $\noise \in (0,1)$ decomposes as:
\[
\acc(k, f, n, \noise) = \suf(k, s(n)) \cdot \rel(k, \noise)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\suf(k, s) \in [0, 1]$ is the \emph{computational sufficiency}---the probability that $k$ steps provide enough computational power to solve the task, with $\suf(k, s) = 1$ for $k \geq s$ and $\suf$ monotone increasing in $k$;
\item $\rel(k, \noise) \in [0, 1]$ is the \emph{reliability}---the probability that a $k$-step chain introduces no fatal error.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The sufficiency function captures the expressivity results of \citet{li2024chain}: below the circuit complexity threshold, the model lacks computational power to solve the task. The reliability function captures empirical noise findings~\cite{zhou2024noisy,liu2025mind}: each step risks introducing errors.

\begin{assumption}[Independent errors]
\label{asm:independent}
Each CoT step independently introduces a fatal error with probability $\noise$, giving $\rel(k, \noise) = (1 - \noise)^k$.
\end{assumption}

We prove our main results under \cref{asm:independent} and discuss extensions to correlated errors in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\begin{assumption}[Sharp sufficiency threshold]
\label{asm:sharp}
The computational sufficiency function has a sharp threshold at the circuit complexity:
\[
\suf(k, s) = \begin{cases}
\beta_0 + (1 - \beta_0) \cdot k/s & \text{if } k < s \\
1 & \text{if } k \geq s
\end{cases}
\]
where $\beta_0 \in [0, 1)$ is the baseline accuracy from heuristics and memorization (achievable without genuine multi-step reasoning).
\end{assumption}

Under these assumptions, the accuracy function is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:accuracy}
\acc(k) = \bigl[\beta_0 + (1 - \beta_0) \min(k/s, 1)\bigr] \cdot (1 - \noise)^k
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Optimal CoT depth]
\label{def:optimal-depth}
The \emph{optimal depth} for task $f$ with circuit complexity $s = s_f(n)$ and per-step error $\noise$ is:
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = \arg\max_{k \geq 0} \; \acc(k, f, n, \noise)
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running example]
\label{ex:running}
Consider three tasks:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Majority} (is the majority of input bits 1?): computable in $\TC$ with circuits of size $O(n)$ and constant depth. A transformer solves this without CoT.
\item \emph{Iterated addition} (sum $n$ numbers in binary): requires $\Theta(n)$ sequential carry propagations. Circuit complexity $s(n) = \Theta(n)$.
\item \emph{Boolean formula evaluation}: $\NC^1$-complete, requiring circuits of polynomial size and $O(\log n)$ depth. Circuit size $s(n) = \poly(n)$ but organized in $O(\log n)$ layers.
\end{itemize}
Our framework predicts $\depth = 0$ for majority, $\depth = \Theta(n/\noise)$ for iterated addition, and $\depth = \Theta(\log n / \noise)$ for boolean formula evaluation (when formalized via circuit depth rather than size).
\end{example}

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Tasks within TC\texorpdfstring{$^0$}{0}: Depth Zero is Optimal}
\label{sec:tc0-bound}

\begin{theorem}[$\TC$ depth bound]
\label{thm:tc0-bound}
Let $f$ be a task computable in $\TC$ by transformer $\mathcal{T}$ without CoT. For any $\noise > 0$:
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = 0
\]
That is, any positive number of CoT steps strictly decreases accuracy.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $f$ is computable by $\mathcal{T}$ in a single forward pass, $\suf(0, s(n)) = 1$. Thus $\acc(0) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1$. For any $k > 0$, $\acc(k) = 1 \cdot (1-\noise)^k < 1$. Therefore $k = 0$ uniquely maximizes accuracy.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\cref{thm:tc0-bound} is intentionally simple---it captures the precise mechanism behind the empirical finding that CoT \emph{hurts} on tasks like pattern matching~\cite{zheng2025curse} and implicit statistical learning~\cite{liu2025mind}. These are tasks that transformers can solve in a single forward pass; adding reasoning steps only introduces noise.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Circuit Complexity Determines Optimal Depth}
\label{sec:scaling}

\begin{theorem}[Depth-complexity scaling]
\label{thm:scaling}
Let $f$ be a task with circuit complexity $s(n) \to \infty$ as $n \to \infty$, and let $\noise \in (0, 1)$ be constant. Under Assumptions~\ref{asm:independent} and~\ref{asm:sharp} with $\beta_0 = 0$ (no baseline heuristic accuracy):
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = s(n)
\]
More generally, for $\beta_0 \in [0, 1)$:
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = s(n) - \frac{(1-\beta_0)}{\noise} + o(1/\noise)
\]
In both cases, $\depth = \Theta(s(n))$ as $n \to \infty$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We analyze the accuracy function from \cref{eq:accuracy} in two regimes.

\emph{Regime 1:} $k < s$. Here $\acc(k) = [\beta_0 + (1-\beta_0)k/s] \cdot (1-\noise)^k$. Taking the log-derivative:
\[
\frac{d}{dk}\log \acc(k) = \frac{(1-\beta_0)/s}{\beta_0 + (1-\beta_0)k/s} + \log(1-\noise)
\]
Setting to zero: the marginal gain from computation equals the marginal loss from noise when
\[
\frac{(1-\beta_0)/s}{\beta_0 + (1-\beta_0)k/s} = -\log(1-\noise) \approx \noise
\]
For $\beta_0 = 0$: $(1-0)/(k) = \noise$, giving $k = 1/\noise$. But this is only the optimum within Regime 1 if $1/\noise < s$, i.e., if $s > 1/\noise$.

\emph{Regime 2:} $k \geq s$. Here $\acc(k) = (1-\noise)^k$, which is strictly decreasing. The maximum in this regime is at $k = s$.

\emph{Combining:} For tasks with $s(n) \to \infty$ (hence eventually $s(n) > 1/\noise$), the global maximum of $\acc(k)$ is at $k = s$ in the $\beta_0 = 0$ case. For $\beta_0 > 0$, the transition is smoother: the model achieves partial accuracy below $s$, so the optimal depth shifts slightly below $s$ where the marginal noise cost of the last few steps exceeds the marginal computational gain. This gives the correction term $-(1-\beta_0)/\noise$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Complexity-conditioned scaling laws]
\label{cor:scaling-laws}
For standard complexity classes (under standard assumptions and $\beta_0 = 0$):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Complexity of $f$} & \textbf{Circuit size $s(n)$} & \textbf{Optimal depth $\depth$} \\
\midrule
$\TC$ & $O(1)$ effective & $0$ \\
$\NC^1 \setminus \TC$ & $\poly(n)$, depth $O(\log n)$ & $\Theta(\log n)$ \\
$\mathrm{P} \setminus \NC^1$ (if nonempty) & $\poly(n)$ & $\Theta(\poly(n))$ \\
$\NP$-complete (worst case) & $2^{\Omega(n)}$ & infeasible \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
For NP-complete tasks, the worst-case circuit size is super-polynomial (under $\mathrm{P} \neq \NP$), making the optimal CoT depth infeasible. In practice, LLMs solve NP instances via heuristics and structural exploitation, corresponding to $\beta_0 > 0$. The framework correctly predicts that CoT provides diminishing returns on NP-hard instances: the gap between heuristic accuracy $\beta_0$ and perfect accuracy cannot be closed by feasible CoT depth.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Noise Ceiling}
\label{sec:noise-ceiling}

\begin{theorem}[Noise ceiling]
\label{thm:noise-ceiling}
For any task $f$ and CoT depth $k > \depth(f, n, \noise)$, the accuracy satisfies:
\[
\acc(k) \leq \acc(\depth) \cdot (1-\noise)^{k - \depth}
\]
with equality when $k \geq s(n)$ (i.e., computational sufficiency is saturated).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $k \geq s(n)$, $\suf(k, s(n)) = 1$ and $\acc(k) = (1-\noise)^k$. Since $\depth \leq s(n)$ by \cref{thm:scaling}:
\[
\acc(k) = (1-\noise)^k = (1-\noise)^{\depth} \cdot (1-\noise)^{k-\depth} \leq \acc(\depth) \cdot (1-\noise)^{k - \depth}
\]
For $k < s(n)$, the inequality holds because $\acc(k) \leq \acc(\depth)$ by definition of $\depth$ as the maximizer, and $(1-\noise)^{k - \depth} \leq 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The noise ceiling provides the formal mechanism behind the ``overthinking'' phenomenon~\cite{hassid2025overthink}: once the model has taken enough steps to solve the task, additional steps can only hurt. The decay rate $(1-\noise)$ per step is universal---it depends only on the model's per-step reliability, not on the task. For $\noise = 0.05$ (a 5\% per-step error rate), accuracy halves every $\log 2 / \noise \approx 14$ extra steps.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Unifying Contradictory Findings}
\label{sec:unification}

\begin{theorem}[Dispute resolution]
\label{thm:unification}
Let $\mathcal{E}_1$ and $\mathcal{E}_2$ be evaluation suites with circuit complexity distributions $S_1$ and $S_2$ respectively. Let $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ be the CoT depth used during evaluation. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $\E_{s \sim S_1}[s] \gg k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ (complex tasks, insufficient depth): increasing $k$ from $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ improves average accuracy.
\item If $\E_{s \sim S_2}[s] \ll k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ (simple tasks, excess depth): increasing $k$ from $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ degrades average accuracy.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For a single task with circuit complexity $s$:
\begin{itemize}
\item If $k_{\mathrm{eval}} < s$, the model is in the rising phase of the inverted U. The derivative $\frac{d}{dk}\acc(k)\big|_{k = k_{\mathrm{eval}}} > 0$.
\item If $k_{\mathrm{eval}} > s$, the model is past the peak. The derivative $\frac{d}{dk}\acc(k)\big|_{k = k_{\mathrm{eval}}} < 0$ (by the noise ceiling).
\end{itemize}
Averaging over the evaluation suite: $\frac{d}{dk}\E_{s \sim S}[\acc(k, s)]$ has the sign of $\E_{s \sim S}[\mathbf{1}(k < s) \cdot g_+(k,s) - \mathbf{1}(k > s) \cdot g_-(k,s)]$ where $g_+, g_-$ are the magnitudes of the positive and negative derivatives. When $\E[s] \gg k$, the positive terms dominate; when $\E[s] \ll k$, the negative terms dominate.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\cref{thm:unification} explains why \citet{yeo2025demystifying} find that longer CoT helps: their evaluation tasks (MATH, AIME, TheoremQA) are high-complexity problems where most models operate below optimal depth. Conversely, \citet{wu2025when} observe the inverted U because their evaluation includes both easy and hard tasks, and their Theorem 4.2 captures the same underlying mechanism through different parameterization. \citet{zheng2025curse} find that direct answering beats CoT on pattern-matching ICL tasks---precisely the $\TC$-solvable tasks where \cref{thm:tc0-bound} predicts $\depth = 0$.
\end{remark}
