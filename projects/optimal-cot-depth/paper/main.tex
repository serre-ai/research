\begin{abstract}
Recent work reaches contradictory conclusions about Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning depth: some studies show longer chains improve performance, while others demonstrate degradation.
We resolve this contradiction through computational complexity theory by proving that the optimal number of reasoning steps is governed by two quantities: the \emph{CoT complexity} $\ccot(n)$ of the task (the minimum steps a transformer needs to solve it) and the model's \emph{per-step error rate} $\noise$.
Our main result establishes that optimal depth satisfies $\depth = \min(\ccot(n),\; \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$, revealing two regimes: an \emph{easy-task regime} where the model can fully solve the problem and optimal depth equals CoT complexity, and a \emph{hard-task regime} where noise accumulation caps useful depth at $1/\noise$ regardless of task complexity.
This \emph{capability ceiling}---the maximum useful reasoning depth is bounded by $1/\noise$, independent of the task---is a genuinely new prediction.
We prove a noise ceiling theorem showing exponential accuracy decay beyond optimal depth, and extend the model to self-correcting reasoners.
Our framework explains why each contradictory finding in the literature is correct for the specific complexity profile of its evaluation tasks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning has become the dominant paradigm for improving the performance of large language models on complex tasks~\cite{wei2022chain,kojima2022large}. By generating intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer, models achieve substantial gains on mathematical, logical, and coding benchmarks. A natural question follows: \emph{how many reasoning steps should a model take?}

Recent work gives contradictory answers. \citet{yeo2025demystifying} show that longer chains of thought---enabled by reinforcement learning---consistently improve performance on competition mathematics, with emergent long CoT patterns outperforming shorter alternatives by over 15 percentage points. In contrast, \citet{wu2025when} demonstrate that accuracy follows an \emph{inverted U-shaped} curve with CoT length: beyond an optimal point, additional reasoning steps degrade performance. They prove this theoretically for a specific task family (Theorem 4.2 of their paper) and validate it empirically across model scales. Meanwhile, \citet{jiang2025safechain} reveal that longer CoT can degrade safety without improving capability, with reasoning models achieving only 32.3\% safety rates despite using 12$\times$ more tokens.

These findings are not contradictory---they reflect an unrecognized confound. We show that the optimal number of reasoning steps is determined by two quantities: the \emph{computational complexity} of the underlying task and the \emph{reliability} of the model's per-step reasoning. This resolves the dispute: longer CoT helps when the task requires computational depth exceeding the transformer's native capacity, and hurts when it does not. Crucially, we identify a \emph{capability ceiling}: no matter how complex the task, useful CoT depth is bounded by $1/\noise$, where $\noise$ is the per-step error rate.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We introduce \emph{CoT complexity} $\ccot_f(n)$---the minimum autoregressive steps a constant-depth transformer needs to solve task $f$---as the natural measure of task difficulty for CoT reasoning, connecting it to circuit complexity via~\citet{li2024chain} (\cref{sec:framework}).

\item We prove the \emph{depth-complexity scaling theorem}: optimal depth satisfies $\depth = \min(\ccot(n),\, \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$, revealing an easy-task regime ($\depth = \ccot(n)$) and a hard-task regime ($\depth = 1/\noise$) separated by the critical threshold $\ccot(n) = 1/\noise$ (\cref{thm:scaling}).

\item We prove a \emph{noise ceiling theorem}: beyond optimal depth, accuracy decays exponentially at rate $(1 - \noise)$ per additional step (\cref{thm:noise-ceiling}).

\item We extend the framework to \emph{self-correcting reasoners}, showing that self-correction raises the ceiling to $1/\neff$ but does not eliminate it unless correction fully dominates error (\cref{prop:self-correction}).

\item We show that the contradiction in the literature dissolves when evaluation tasks are classified by CoT complexity (\cref{cor:dispute}).
\end{enumerate}

Our results build on Li et al.'s~\cite{li2024chain} foundational result that $T$ CoT steps grant a constant-depth transformer the power of boolean circuits of size $T$, and on lower bounds by \citet{amiri2025lower} and \citet{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}. Relative to the closest prior work---\citet{wu2025when}, who prove optimal CoT length via abstract difficulty and capability parameters---our framework grounds these parameters in computational complexity, yielding the new prediction that useful depth saturates at $1/\noise$ for hard tasks. This capability ceiling is absent from their analysis.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} CoT prompting~\cite{wei2022chain} and its zero-shot variant~\cite{kojima2022large} have driven substantial gains in LLM reasoning. The debate over optimal CoT length has intensified: pro-length findings on complex tasks~\cite{yeo2025demystifying}, anti-length evidence on simpler or pattern-based tasks~\cite{wu2025when,hassid2025overthink,zheng2025curse,liu2025mind}, and efficiency-focused work seeking the right balance~\cite{yang2025thinking,chen2026deep}. We unify these findings under a complexity-theoretic framework.

\paragraph{Complexity of transformers with CoT.} Without CoT, constant-depth transformers compute functions in $\TC$~\cite{merrill2022saturated} or $\mathrm{AC}^0$ under bounded precision~\cite{li2024chain}. With CoT, $T$ steps enable circuits of size $T$~\cite{li2024chain}, $O(n)$ steps capture regular languages, and $\poly(n)$ steps capture exactly $\mathrm{P}$~\cite{merrill2024expressive}. Lower bounds show parity requires linear CoT~\cite{amiri2025lower}, and the Ehrenfeucht-Haussler rank exactly characterizes minimum CoT steps for function composition~\cite{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}. These results establish \emph{what} CoT can compute; we address \emph{how much} CoT is optimal in the presence of noise.

\paragraph{Error accumulation in reasoning.} Noisy rationales degrade CoT performance by up to 40\%~\cite{zhou2024noisy}, with inaccurate intermediate steps far more damaging than irrelevant ones. CoT can reduce performance by 36\% on tasks where deliberation hurts humans~\cite{liu2025mind}. These findings motivate our noise model but lack formal connection to task complexity.

\paragraph{Optimal CoT length theory.} Most closely related, \citet{wu2025when} prove that optimal CoT length satisfies $N^*(M,T) = TZ/\bigl(M(Z+1)\bigr)$ where $T$ and $M$ are abstract difficulty and capability parameters (Theorem 4.2). Our framework makes two advances: (i)~we ground $T$ in CoT complexity $\ccot(n)$, which connects to circuit complexity via~\citet{li2024chain} and admits known tight bounds for specific problems~\cite{amiri2025lower,barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}; (ii)~we identify the \emph{capability ceiling} $\depth \leq 1/\noise$---a regime where optimal depth is determined entirely by model reliability, independent of task complexity. This ceiling is absent from Wu et al.'s analysis, where $N^*$ grows without bound as $T \to \infty$.

\section{Formal Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

We formalize CoT reasoning as sequential computation and define the optimal depth function by decomposing accuracy into two competing factors.

\begin{definition}[Reasoning task]
\label{def:task}
A \emph{reasoning task} is a family of functions $f = \{f_n\}_{n \in \N}$ where $f_n : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$. The \emph{circuit complexity} of $f$ is the function $s_f : \N \to \N$ where $s_f(n)$ is the minimum boolean circuit size computing $f_n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[CoT execution]
\label{def:cot}
Let $\mathcal{T}$ be a constant-depth transformer with $L$ layers, embedding dimension $d = O(\log n)$, and precision $p$ bits. A \emph{CoT execution of depth $k$} on input $x \in \{0,1\}^n$ is a sequence of autoregressive generation steps:
\[
x = z_0 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_1 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_2 \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} \cdots \xrightarrow{\mathcal{T}} z_k
\]
where each $z_i$ extends the context with one generated token. The final token $z_k$ encodes the answer.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[CoT complexity]
\label{def:cot-complexity}
The \emph{CoT complexity} of a task $f$ is the function $\ccot_f : \N \to \N$ where $\ccot_f(n)$ is the minimum number of autoregressive steps for which there exists a constant-depth transformer that correctly computes $f_n$ on all inputs of size $n$.
\end{definition}

CoT complexity is the natural task-difficulty measure in our framework. It is bounded above by circuit complexity but can be substantially smaller, since each CoT step involves a full forward pass through the transformer (providing parallel computation within that step).

\begin{proposition}[Bounds on CoT complexity]
\label{prop:cot-bounds}
For any task $f$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\ccot_f(n) \leq s_f(n)$ \emph{(circuit size bound, \citet{li2024chain}):} $T$ CoT steps suffice for any function with circuits of size $T$.
\item $\ccot_f(n) = 0$ for $f \in \TC$ \emph{(\citet{merrill2022saturated}):} tasks within the transformer's native class need no CoT.
\item $\ccot_f(n) = \Theta(n)$ for parity \emph{(\citet{amiri2025lower}):} tight lower and upper bounds.
\item $\ccot_f(n) = l$ for $l$-fold function composition \emph{(\citet{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}):} exact characterization via Ehrenfeucht-Haussler rank.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

We now decompose accuracy into computational sufficiency and reliability.

\begin{definition}[Accuracy decomposition]
\label{def:accuracy}
The accuracy of a CoT execution of depth $k$ on task $f$ with CoT complexity $\ccot(n)$ and per-step error probability $\noise \in (0,1)$ decomposes as:
\[
\acc(k, f, n, \noise) = \suf(k, \ccot(n)) \cdot \rel(k, \noise)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\suf(k, c) \in [0, \Smax]$ is the \emph{computational sufficiency}---the expected accuracy from computation alone, given $k$ steps and CoT complexity $c$, with $\suf$ monotone increasing in $k$;
\item $\rel(k, \noise) \in [0, 1]$ is the \emph{reliability}---the probability that a $k$-step chain introduces no fatal error.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[On the decomposition]
\label{rem:decomposition}
The multiplicative decomposition is a modeling assumption, not a theorem. It posits that ``sufficient computation'' and ``no fatal errors'' are independent events. This is an idealization: in practice, errors may correlate with computational difficulty. We adopt it for analytical tractability; our qualitative results (the inverted U, the capability ceiling) hold under weaker assumptions, as we discuss in \cref{sec:discussion}.
\end{remark}

The sufficiency function captures an important distinction between \emph{expressivity} and \emph{capability}. By \citet{li2024chain}, $k \geq s_f(n)$ CoT steps make the transformer \emph{expressive enough} to compute $f$. But expressivity does not guarantee that a trained model will find the correct computation~\cite{kim2025parity}. We therefore bound sufficiency by $\Smax \leq 1$, representing the maximum accuracy a trained model achieves on task $f$ given sufficient steps.

\begin{assumption}[Independent errors]
\label{asm:independent}
Each CoT step independently introduces a fatal error with probability $\noise$, giving $\rel(k, \noise) = (1 - \noise)^k$.
\end{assumption}

We prove our main results under \cref{asm:independent} and extend to self-correcting models in \cref{prop:self-correction}.

\begin{assumption}[Linear sufficiency]
\label{asm:linear}
The computational sufficiency function increases linearly up to the CoT complexity threshold:
\[
\suf(k, c) = \begin{cases}
\Smax & \text{if } c = 0 \text{ (task in $\TC$)} \\
\Smax \cdot \min\!\left(\dfrac{k}{c},\; 1\right) & \text{if } c \geq 1
\end{cases}
\]
where $\Smax \in (0, 1]$ is the maximum achievable sufficiency and $c = \ccot_f(n)$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[Baseline accuracy]
\label{rem:baseline}
For tasks with $c \geq 1$, the assumption gives $\suf(0, c) = 0$: the model achieves zero accuracy without CoT. This is an idealization---real models achieve nonzero baseline accuracy via memorization and heuristics. We adopt it because $\Smax$ cancels from the optimal depth: $\depth = \arg\max_k \acc(k) = \arg\max_k [\min(k/c, 1) \cdot (1-\noise)^k]$, which is independent of $\Smax$. Adding a baseline term $\beta_0 > 0$ shifts $\depth$ downward slightly (the model needs fewer CoT steps because it starts with partial accuracy) but preserves the capability ceiling at $1/\noise$. We treat $\beta_0 = 0$ for analytical clarity; see \cref{app:proofs} for the general case.
\end{remark}

For tasks with $c \geq 1$, this gives the accuracy function:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:accuracy}
\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot \min\!\left(\frac{k}{c},\; 1\right) \cdot (1 - \noise)^k
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Optimal CoT depth]
\label{def:optimal-depth}
The \emph{optimal depth} for task $f$ with CoT complexity $c = \ccot_f(n)$ and per-step error $\noise$ is:
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = \arg\max_{k \geq 0} \; \acc(k, f, n, \noise)
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Running example]
\label{ex:running}
Consider three tasks with known CoT complexity:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Majority} (is the majority of input bits 1?): $\ccot(n) = 0$, solvable in $\TC$ without CoT.
\item \emph{Parity} (is the XOR of all input bits 1?): $\ccot(n) = \Theta(n)$~\cite{amiri2025lower}, requires linear CoT.
\item \emph{5-fold function composition} (compute $f \circ f \circ f \circ f \circ f$): $\ccot = 5$ exactly~\cite{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht}.
\end{itemize}
For a model with $\noise = 0.05$ (so $1/\noise = 20$): our framework predicts $\depth = 0$ for majority, $\depth = \min(n, 20) = 20$ for parity with $n > 20$ (noise-capped---the model cannot solve large parity instances), and $\depth = \min(5, 20) = 5$ for 5-fold composition (fully solvable).
\end{example}

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Tasks within TC\texorpdfstring{$^0$}{0}: CoT Can Only Hurt}
\label{sec:tc0-bound}

\begin{theorem}[$\TC$ depth bound]
\label{thm:tc0-bound}
Let $f$ be a task with $\ccot_f(n) = 0$ (i.e., solvable by $\mathcal{T}$ without CoT). For any $\noise > 0$:
\[
\depth(f, n, \noise) = 0
\]
That is, any positive number of CoT steps strictly decreases accuracy.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\ccot_f(n) = 0$, we have $\suf(k, 0) = \Smax$ for all $k \geq 0$. Thus $\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot (1-\noise)^k$, which is strictly decreasing in $k$ for $\noise > 0$. The unique maximizer is $k = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\cref{thm:tc0-bound} does not assume perfect accuracy---$\Smax$ may be well below 1. The result says that \emph{whatever accuracy the model achieves without CoT, adding reasoning steps can only make it worse} on tasks the model can already solve in a single forward pass. This captures the empirical finding that CoT hurts on pattern matching~\cite{zheng2025curse} and implicit statistical learning~\cite{liu2025mind}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Depth-Complexity Scaling Theorem}
\label{sec:scaling}

\begin{theorem}[Depth-complexity scaling]
\label{thm:scaling}
Let $f$ be a task with CoT complexity $c = \ccot_f(n) \geq 1$, and let $\noise \in (0, 1)$. Define $k^* = -1/\!\ln(1-\noise)$, the unique maximizer of $k \mapsto k \cdot (1 - \noise)^k$. Under Assumptions~\ref{asm:independent} and~\ref{asm:linear}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\emph{(a)}] \textbf{Easy-task regime} ($c \leq k^*$, equivalently $\noise \cdot c \leq 1$ for small $\noise$): $\depth = c$. The model can fully solve the task; optimal depth equals CoT complexity.
\item[\emph{(b)}] \textbf{Hard-task regime} ($c > k^*$, equivalently $\noise \cdot c > 1$): $\depth = \lfloor k^* \rfloor \approx \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor$. Noise caps useful depth before sufficient computation is reached.
\end{enumerate}
In both cases:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main-result}
\depth(f, n, \noise) = \min\bigl(c,\; \lfloor k^* \rfloor\bigr) = \min\!\left(\ccot_f(n),\; \left\lfloor \frac{-1}{\ln(1 - \noise)} \right\rfloor\right)
\end{equation}
For small $\noise$: $\depth \approx \min(\ccot_f(n),\; \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
From \cref{eq:accuracy}, the accuracy function has two regimes.

\emph{Regime 1} ($k < c$): $\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot \frac{k}{c} \cdot (1 - \noise)^k$. Up to the positive constant $\Smax/c$, we are maximizing $h(k) = k \cdot (1 - \noise)^k$. Taking the derivative:
\[
h'(k) = (1-\noise)^k\bigl[1 + k \ln(1-\noise)\bigr]
\]
Setting $h'(k) = 0$ gives $k^* = -1/\ln(1 - \noise)$. Since $h''(k^*) < 0$, this is the unique maximum of $h$ on $(0, \infty)$. For small $\noise$, $k^* \approx 1/\noise$.

\emph{Regime 2} ($k \geq c$): $\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot (1 - \noise)^k$, which is strictly decreasing. The maximum in this regime is at $k = c$.

\emph{Case (a):} $c \leq k^*$. In Regime 1, $h$ is increasing on $[0, k^*]$, so $h$ is increasing on $[0, c]$. The Regime 1 maximum restricted to $k \in [0, c)$ approaches $h(c)$ from below. The Regime 2 maximum is at $k = c$ with $\acc(c) = \Smax \cdot (1-\noise)^c$. The Regime 1 value at $k \to c^-$ is $\Smax \cdot \frac{c}{c} \cdot (1-\noise)^c = \acc(c)$. By continuity, $\acc$ is maximized at $k = c$, and $\depth = c$.

\emph{Case (b):} $c > k^*$. The Regime 1 maximum is at $k^*$ with $\acc(k^*) = \Smax \cdot \frac{k^*}{c} \cdot (1-\noise)^{k^*}$. The Regime 2 maximum is at $k = c$ with $\acc(c) = \Smax \cdot (1-\noise)^c$. We compare:
\[
\frac{\acc(k^*)}{\acc(c)} = \frac{k^*}{c} \cdot (1-\noise)^{k^* - c} = \frac{k^*}{c} \cdot \left(\frac{1}{1-\noise}\right)^{c - k^*}
\]
Taking logarithms with $x = c/k^* > 1$:
\begin{align*}
\ln\frac{\acc(k^*)}{\acc(c)} &= \ln\frac{k^*}{c} + (c - k^*)\ln\frac{1}{1-\noise} \\
&= -\ln x + (x - 1) \cdot k^* \cdot \bigl(-\ln(1-\noise)\bigr) \\
&= -\ln x + (x - 1) \tag{since $k^* = -1/\ln(1-\noise)$}
\end{align*}
For $x > 1$: $x - 1 > \ln x$ (strict convexity of $e^t$), so $\ln(\acc(k^*)/\acc(c)) > 0$. Therefore $\acc(k^*) > \acc(c)$ whenever $c > k^*$, and the continuous optimum is at $k^*$.

\emph{Discretization:} Since $k$ must be a non-negative integer, $\depth \in \{\lfloor k^* \rfloor, \lceil k^* \rceil\}$. For small $\noise$, $k^* \approx 1/\noise - 1/2$, and $h(\lfloor k^* \rfloor) = h(\lceil k^* \rceil)$ to leading order (the ratio is $1 - \noise^2 + O(\noise^3) \approx 1$). We write $\depth = \lfloor k^* \rfloor$; either choice changes optimal depth by at most 1.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The capability ceiling]
\label{rem:ceiling}
The hard-task regime reveals a \emph{capability ceiling}: $\depth \leq \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor$, regardless of task complexity. A model with 5\% per-step error ($\noise = 0.05$) has a ceiling of $\sim\!20$ useful reasoning steps. Beyond this, noise overwhelms any computational benefit from additional steps. Stronger models (lower $\noise$) can reason deeper, but every model has a finite horizon. This is the key prediction absent from \citet{wu2025when}: their $N^* = TZ/(M(Z+1))$ grows without bound as $T \to \infty$, whereas our $\depth$ saturates.

Note that $\depth$ depends only on $\ccot(n)$ and $\noise$---not on $\Smax$. Model quality (captured by $\Smax$) determines peak \emph{accuracy} but not optimal \emph{depth}. The capability ceiling $1/\noise$ is purely a function of per-step reliability.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Scaling laws for specific problems]
\label{cor:scaling-laws}
For specific problem families with known CoT complexity:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Problem family} & \textbf{CoT complexity $\ccot(n)$} & \textbf{Source} & \textbf{$\depth$} \\
\midrule
Majority, comparison & $0$ & \cite{merrill2022saturated} & $0$ \\
$l$-fold composition & $l$ & \cite{barcelo2025ehrenfeucht} & $\min(l, \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$ \\
Parity & $\Theta(n)$ & \cite{amiri2025lower} & $\min(n, \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$ \\
Multiplication & $\geq \alpha n$ & \cite{amiri2025lower} & $\geq \min(\alpha n, \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$ \\
Any $f \in \SIZE(s)$ & $\leq s$ & \cite{li2024chain} & $\min(s, \lfloor 1/\noise \rfloor)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
For any problem with CoT complexity growing faster than $1/\noise$, optimal depth is determined by the model, not the task.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{The Noise Ceiling}
\label{sec:noise-ceiling}

\begin{theorem}[Noise ceiling]
\label{thm:noise-ceiling}
For any task $f$ with CoT complexity $c \geq 1$ and any CoT depth $k \geq c$:
\[
\acc(k) = \acc(c) \cdot (1-\noise)^{k - c}
\]
That is, once computational sufficiency is reached, each additional step reduces accuracy by exactly $(1 - \noise)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $k \geq c$, sufficiency is saturated: $\suf(k, c) = \Smax$. Thus $\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot (1-\noise)^k$. Since $\acc(c) = \Smax \cdot (1-\noise)^c$, dividing gives $\acc(k)/\acc(c) = (1-\noise)^{k-c}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The noise ceiling has two consequences. First, it is \emph{universal}: once sufficient steps are taken, the decay rate $(1-\noise)$ per step depends only on the model's per-step reliability, not on the task. For $\noise = 0.05$, accuracy halves every $\lceil\ln 2 / \noise\rceil = 14$ excess steps. This provides the formal mechanism behind the ``overthinking'' phenomenon~\cite{hassid2025overthink}. Second, combined with \cref{thm:scaling}, the noise ceiling implies that $\acc$ is unimodal: it increases toward $\depth$ (rising sufficiency) and decreases beyond $c$ (exponential noise decay). In the hard-task regime where $\depth < c$, accuracy peaks at $\depth$ and then \emph{continues to rise} in the interval $(\depth, c)$ (sufficiency still growing) before the exponential decay sets in at $k \geq c$---but this secondary rise never exceeds $\acc(\depth)$, as shown in the proof of \cref{thm:scaling}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Self-Correcting Reasoners}
\label{sec:self-correction}

Modern reasoning models (e.g., o1, DeepSeek-R1) explicitly backtrack and correct errors during CoT. \cref{asm:independent} does not capture this. We extend the framework.

\begin{proposition}[Self-correction extension]
\label{prop:self-correction}
Suppose each CoT step independently introduces a fatal error with probability $\noise$ and, conditional on an error having occurred, corrects it within the same step with probability $\gamma \in [0, 1)$. The effective per-step error rate is $\neff = \noise(1 - \gamma)$, and the depth-complexity scaling theorem (\cref{thm:scaling}) holds with $\noise$ replaced by $\neff$:
\[
\depth = \min\!\left(\ccot(n),\; \left\lfloor \frac{1}{\neff} \right\rfloor\right) = \min\!\left(\ccot(n),\; \left\lfloor \frac{1}{\noise(1-\gamma)} \right\rfloor\right)
\]
Self-correction raises the capability ceiling from $1/\noise$ to $1/(\noise(1-\gamma))$ but does not eliminate it for $\gamma < 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under this model, each step produces a fatal error that persists with probability $\noise(1-\gamma) = \neff$. Errors across steps remain independent (each step's correction applies only to its own error). Thus $\rel(k) = (1-\neff)^k$, and the proof of \cref{thm:scaling} applies verbatim with $\noise \to \neff$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For a model with $\noise = 0.10$ and $\gamma = 0.5$ (corrects half its errors): $\neff = 0.05$, ceiling $= 20$ steps. Without self-correction: ceiling $= 10$ steps. Self-correction doubles useful depth but does not make it unbounded. The $\gamma \to 1$ limit (perfect correction) is unreachable: correcting an error requires recognizing it, which itself may fail.

\cref{prop:self-correction} models \emph{local} error suppression---careful reasoning that avoids mistakes within a single step. Modern reasoning models (o1, DeepSeek-R1) also exhibit \emph{multi-step backtracking}: generating a wrong sub-answer, recognizing the error several steps later, and revising. This global correction process is not captured by per-step $\gamma$, as it introduces non-monotone reliability (R may dip and recover). Modeling backtracking formally requires a richer framework, such as a random walk on error states with position-dependent correction rates, and is an important direction for future work.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Resolving the CoT Length Dispute}
\label{sec:unification}

\begin{corollary}[Dispute resolution]
\label{cor:dispute}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be an evaluation suite with CoT complexity distribution $\ccot \sim S$ over tasks. Let $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ be the CoT depth used during evaluation. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $k_{\mathrm{eval}} < \E_{c \sim S}[\min(c, 1/\noise)]$: increasing $k$ from $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ improves average accuracy (``more is better'' regime).
\item If $k_{\mathrm{eval}} > \E_{c \sim S}[\min(c, 1/\noise)]$: increasing $k$ from $k_{\mathrm{eval}}$ degrades average accuracy (``more is worse'' regime).
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Each task's accuracy $\acc(k)$ is unimodal with peak at $\depth = \min(c, 1/\noise)$. The average $\E_S[\acc(k)]$ is a mixture of unimodal functions. Below the average peak, the increasing components dominate; above, the decreasing components dominate.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This explains the dispute: \citet{yeo2025demystifying} evaluate on MATH, AIME, and TheoremQA---tasks with high CoT complexity where most models operate below optimal depth (``more is better''). \citet{wu2025when} observe the inverted U on a mixture of easy and hard tasks. \citet{zheng2025curse} find that direct answering outperforms CoT on pattern-matching ICL tasks---precisely the $\TC$-solvable tasks where \cref{thm:tc0-bound} predicts $\depth = 0$. Each finding is correct for the complexity profile of its evaluation suite.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Generalization to Nonzero Baseline ($\beta_0 > 0$)}
\label{app:baseline}

When the model achieves baseline accuracy $\beta_0 > 0$ without CoT (via memorization or heuristics), the sufficiency function becomes:
\[
\suf(k, c) = \Smax \cdot \bigl[\beta_0 + (1 - \beta_0)\min(k/c, 1)\bigr]
\]
The accuracy in Regime 1 ($k < c$) is:
\[
\acc(k) = \Smax \cdot \bigl[\beta_0 + (1-\beta_0)k/c\bigr] \cdot (1-\noise)^k
\]
Setting the derivative to zero:
\[
\frac{(1-\beta_0)/c}{\beta_0 + (1-\beta_0)k/c} = -\ln(1-\noise) \approx \noise
\]
Solving: $k^*_{\beta} = 1/\noise - c\beta_0/(1-\beta_0)$. The capability ceiling shifts from $1/\noise$ to $1/\noise - c\beta_0/(1-\beta_0)$, which is \emph{lower}: a model with strong baseline needs fewer CoT steps. The two-regime structure and the existence of the ceiling are preserved.
